One — the dress hanging

It hung from the top of the closet door on a padded hanger he must have owned for the purpose, and for a second she just stood in the doorway with her keys still in her hand and looked at it.

It didn't look like a thing a person made. That was the first thing — it looked finished, store-finished, the kind of object that arrives already complete from somewhere with a name, and the knowledge that it had been a flat bolt of cloth on his table two weeks ago and a pinned rough shape around her body since then sat strangely against the clean fall of it now. The burgundy drank the lamplight and gave a little of it back along one long fold, a sheen that moved when the door moved. One shoulder rose to a clean covered strap; the other side ran bare, the neckline crossing on a diagonal from the high shoulder down toward the opposite arm, so the whole dress leaned, asked the eye to travel it on a slant instead of straight across. There was a slit she could see even on the hanger, set into the bare side, running higher than the dresses she owned ran.

Her hand came up to her collarbone.

It was beautiful. She knew it was beautiful the way she'd have known it on someone else, in a magazine, on a girl built to wear exactly this and unembarrassed about it — and right behind the knowing, before she could stop it, came the small interior recoil, the voice that looked at the bare shoulder and the lean of the neckline and the height of that slit and said, quietly, in her mother's register, who wears that. The kind of girl who wanted to be looked at. The kind who'd built a whole night around being looked at.
He'd made it for her. He'd matched the color to her own skin and spent two weeks on it and pinned it to the shape of her on his knees, and the dress he'd made — the dress that was hers — was the kind of dress she'd spent her whole life filing under that. The two facts would not lie down next to each other. She stood there holding both and feeling the heat come up into her face in the empty room, looking at a beautiful thing she already half-believed she shouldn't be allowed to want this much.
She wanted it so much her chest hurt.

Two — putting it on, the mirror

The silk went over her head and came down her body like water finding its level — cool for the first half-second and then not cool, taking her own warmth back almost at once, lying against her skin everywhere it touched with a weight she felt in the backs of her arms, along her ribs, low over her hip. She'd worn dresses. She had never worn anything that held her like this, that knew where she was. He'd built it knowing where she was. Every place it lay close was a place his hands had measured, and her body knew that before her mind said it, the skin remembering being read
.
She reached back for the closure and there was almost nothing to do — it sat, it closed, it fit. No fighting a zipper, no tugging anything into place. It just settled, like it had been waiting.

She turned to the mirror.

The bare shoulder was the first thing — her own shoulder, rounder and warmer than she thought of it, and the long diagonal of the neckline drawing down across her, and the burgundy doing to her coloring exactly what it had done in the dark window the day he'd held the cloth to her face: making her look like she'd been mixed on purpose, the red of her hair and the warmth of her skin and the deep wine of the silk all agreeing with each other. The dress took her width and made it a line. Her hips, her waist, the full of her chest — the diagonal gathered all of it and pulled it long, so that the abundance she was always faintly bracing for in mirrors read, here, as much in the good way, as a lot of woman the dress was glad to have.

Then she moved, to see, and the slit opened along her thigh and shut again, and there it was. The whole length of her leg for half a step and then gone. Her stomach dropped the small dropping way.

Floozy, said the voice, prompt, certain.

She stood still and made herself look anyway. Because the woman in the glass — the bare shoulder, the lean of the neckline, the slit that gave her leg away when she walked — the woman in the glass looked like the kind of girl who got looked at and knew it, who'd dressed for exactly that and wasn't sorry, and the terrible thing, the thing she could feel low in her body even with her mother's word still hanging in the air, was that she wanted to be that woman. She wanted to walk into the induction in this dress and have the slit open and have people see her leg and not apologize for one second of it.
She put her hand to her collarbone, against the bare skin where the neckline left it, and watched the woman in the mirror do the same thing, and the woman in the mirror looked, for once, like she was getting away with something.
