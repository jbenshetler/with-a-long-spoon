## The object and the frame

- Klimt's *Danaë* (c. 1907) — a redheaded woman in golden ecstasy. Obscure enough that most readers would have to look it up; the obscurity is the deniability (surface: a tasteful gold nude; the charge invisible to anyone who doesn't know the painting).
- A **giclée** print Pace **buys on a conference trip**, in a **frame he builds** in the woodshop. The gift lands in **summer** — the dig starves their time (Vee and Randi buried in Marsh's two-site fieldwork; Pace home year-round, wanting more of her than the dig allows) — and Vee hangs it in **their bedroom**.
- **The frame is containment.** A mitered frame whose corners must be true to better than 0.1° or the seam shows: he builds the container for the image of her own possession *whose whole virtue is that no seam shows — the same virtue the scheme requires.* Rhymes the made-to-you dress, the no-tag shirt, the bench, the key box: his hands, his precision, tenderness and containment indistinguishable. **Guard: never state it; render the frame as faultless.**

## The myth-substrate (deniable, never explained on the page)

Why the image fits the engine — the reader does this work, the text never does:

- **Gold rain = invisible possession, and money as its medium** (Randi's careless multiples, the credit card, money-as-power).
- **Reached through the one gap** — Danaë locked in the bronze tower, the god entering through the single unguarded opening = Pace's consent architecture and its hole (guards against force, never against deceived consent).
- **Solitary ecstasy = Vee's shame engine** (the body wanting without permission, displayed).

## The resemblance guard (two readings; only one stays off the page)

- **Surface resemblance — Vee DOES note it.** A sensual, gold, abundant nude redhead, given by her lover: a redheaded woman in a deeply sensual relationship noting *"that's how he sees me"* is the **gift landing** — warm, body-first, not a deduction. Suppressing it makes the gift inert. Her happy **"that's me"** — embracing the image of her own possession as a love-token — **is the knife on reread.** The warmth is the tragedy later; suppress it and you remove the knife.
- **Mythic charge — never narrated, and never reached by Vee.** The Zeus / possession / deception allegory stays off the page, naturally: a woman in love reads a lover's gift as romance, not prophecy; nobody allegorizes a gift against themselves. Cleanest if she responds to the *image as her*, not to the specific myth.

**The guard, precisely: resemblance yes (warm, body-first); myth-as-allegory-of-her never narrated, never reached by Vee.**

## "God among men" — the Zeus plant

Randi, hearing Vee gush about her sex life with Pace, caps it with a **bawdy joke: *"He is a god among men."*** Double register: warm friend-hype on first read; knowing appraisal, from a woman who's had him, on reread. **Guards:** sourced to Vee's own gushing, so firsthand knowledge stays deniable; **one memorable beat, not a campaign** (a campaign reads as Randi selling Pace — telegraph). With *Danaë* on the wall the substrate snaps together — Pace the god, Vee taken through the door she left unguarded — but only if delivered without knowing *weight* and never explained: the danger is the weight, not the line; the standing guard is **never-explain**. Plant early (a V1/V2 brunch). It is an SATC beat homed here because it arms this substrate.

## Abandoned, not reclaimed

At the exit Vee **leaves the painting behind** — seen, not retrieved (see `meta-note-threesome-reveal.md` → the exit). *Danaë* is **his** claim on her body; the green sheets are **her** claim on his space; she leaves both — renouncing possession **in both directions.**

## The two poles (coexistence with Bella Donna)

*Danaë* does **not** replace the dorm's *Bella Donna* (Stevie Nicks) — **two poles, two rooms.** Bella Donna is the **dorm pole**, Danaë the **house pole**; both run the book's youthful-surface / mature-underneath double-read (Stevie's lyrics carry it in the music medium as Klimt carries it in paint) — the read-twice, planted twice. Full dorm-poster rendering: `meta-craft-vivienne.md`.

## End-matter (ebook)

The actual *Danaë* image as end matter, with a **reverse link** to where it appears in the book.

- **Spare, uncaptioned:** the image + *"Gustav Klimt,* Danaë *(c. 1907)"* + the reverse link. **No interpretive gloss** — explaining the resonance hands over the exegesis the novel withholds and collapses the method.
- **Reverse link only** (end → scene). **No forward link** at the gift scene; keep the scene clean, and let discovery happen by *finishing*.
- **Low spoiler precisely because unglossed** — a reader paging early sees "a painting that appears in Ch. X," curiosity not catastrophe; the caption is what would spoil, and there is none.
- **Rationale:** the reader gets the full image **at the end**, then can go back and re-see — reproducing the book's engine, where the reveal hands Vee the **full dataset at the end.** The theme in the book's physical shape.
- **Practical:** *Danaë* is public domain (Klimt d. 1918); source a faithful high-res PD reproduction (Wikimedia / Google Arts & Culture) to avoid reproduction-rights claims. Keep end matter **Danaë-only** (Bella Donna is an in-copyright photo; also focus).
