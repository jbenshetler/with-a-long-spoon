# Condensed — Under the Rug

*Scene (triptych) · Draft complete · `under-the-rug.md` · date owned by chronology · Pace/Vee · Vee POV (close third) throughout*

A diptych-grown-triptych across three rooms — bedroom, restaurant, bedroom — in which a displaced fight is dissolved on the surface and the real thing is buried deeper. The proxy gets resolved (warmly, even); that false resolution is the point. Sets up His Shirt — Pre-Dawn (the failed release is why the shapelessness then goes unbearable) and Believe Me.

## The engine

Vee is the happiest she's ever been, and the happiness wants a name — the three words his vow forbids (he says it first; he hasn't). The real thing is never spoken in this unit; it only ghosts. Three displacements of the same want across the arc: **this ("just sex," furthest from the real thing) → His Shirt (wordless, alone) → Believe Me ("what are you to me," closest, still mismatched).**

The accusation is **manufactured on the spot** — no standing grievance. It runs on *fear*, not a rule: she wants to say it, is afraid it comes back empty, feels her heart out ahead of whatever this actually is, and suspects the sex is blinding her. Crucially that self-suspicion is played as **ordinary infatuation-anxiety** (*am I deluding myself that this is love*), zero detective energy — the reader, holding the orchestration since chapter 2, hears the literal mechanism (the body used to cloud judgment) while she means only a crush. The dance-callback ties this to the CW-Dance fight, where she also wrongly suspected him and apologized: a pattern that inoculates her against the one cynical instinct that's true.

## Panel 1 — bedroom (his house), post-sex

Post-coital. The want curdles into a "just sex" charge, displaced through *we never go out* (pegged to the dance as the last time out — canon-accurate; next public outing isn't till March). She leaks: the line comes out too loud, too sharp for the room, and she knows it. Pace's reply is **delayed — engaged concentration, not defense** (the wood-knot / proof stillness), then simple and warm: *"Okay. Where do you want to go tonight?"* The accommodation disarms the fight she didn't consciously know she wanted; she fumes at the calm, finds nothing to push against. She names the first place her mouth finds — a kitschy chain (Chili's out by 460), reaching for the gloppy nachos, *the opposite of anything he'd ever set in front of her* — which quietly inverts the Porch's cracker-and-meal figure (she reaches for the crackers), reread-only, never named. The TVs/noise are planted on entry in Panel 2, not in her naming. His accommodation is the wall; he's not over-analytical yet — a man solving a stated problem, warmly. The three words stay off the page (Porch treatment: *the word*, *the sentence*, never printed).

## Panel 2 — restaurant (TVs on three walls, music)

The drive over: **he makes easy small talk in the quiet car, she answers curtly** (not rude, not engaging) — which strengthens the reveal by contrast (verbally present in the low-stimulus car, gone in the loud room; no narration needed). At the table, sensory overload breaks through, rendered in behavior only, neurodivergence **never named**: eyes pulled past her face to the screen behind her, the fork turned and squared, *"Sorry, say it again?"*, and — uncharacteristically — a flicker of plain irritation she's never seen on him. The irony hinges: **his distress is timed to the room, not to her** (eyes slip on screen-motion, irritation arrives on the song-change), and the dropped-eyes read as *shielding* her from it — but Vee runs the opposite correlation and lands on *"irritated. With her,"* the just-sex charge "proven." The **nachos epiphany**: a food she *had liked* — *fine*, and fine had been plenty — now dead in her mouth (callback to Cassie — School Nights' *the men before were fine, Pace is not fine*; one italicized *fine*, the word carries the relationship ruin, no men named). His cooking by contrast gives each ingredient its voice; the factory plate is every color stirred to one muddy brown (painting figure, quietly rhymes Still Life). *"He had ruined it for her… Not just this"* — the only aperture onto ordinary-relationships, never wider. Ends in sullen silence, joyless chewing. (Close third Vee keeps the reader ahead.)

## Panel 3 — back home (bedroom)

**The truck → house → second fight → false resolution.** In the truck Pace brightens (sensory relief — the third reading of the same man, completing the contrast); Vee misreads it as relief at escaping the outing she demanded, half-senses the wrong note, doesn't dig (the under-the-rug reflex). Needing him more than he seems to need her, the need makes her mean. At the house he reads her for what she wants; her restlessness snaps into the apex misread — a *you-punish-me* pattern stacked from three misread data points (CW bar, restaurant, truck), the CW-bar one a flat distortion she half-knows is false (she was the cold one; she carried the Humble Pie up his porch steps). Pace doesn't litigate it — one quiet merciful line, *"You know what happened after the dance,"* set down, not argued (it answers her own back-room knowing). Her case collapses; the quiet truth shames her. Then he gives the real thing, **un-over-explained, neurodivergence never named**: the restaurant was *too loud — not the sound, the whole of it* — *"I listen as hard as I know how and I still can't hear you… I'm listening now."* The hear/listen figure works because it's **literally true** (fact first, meaning second); *"I'm listening now"* is the **vow-substitute** — devotion through attention, the deed-channel "I love you" without the forbidden words — and it refutes *just sex* by making her someone he listens to, a person not a body.

**The close.** Vee **relents** — not grace, not forgiveness earned, but reprieve from fear, sympathy for him, and dread of a life without him; she miscategorizes it to herself as *forgiveness* (free indirect, *forgiven* in her own warm thought — the self-deception visible only in the pattern and the coda, never narrated). Makeup sex, **conventional, warm, relief-charged, no shame-lighting** — the warmth is real, and the warmth is the rug. Structure: his thorough drawing-out (mouth → neck → lingering at her breasts, *him taking comfort from her* → oral, her first orgasm on his mouth) set against her **impatience for his control to break** — the load-bearing symbol, never named: she wants him to give in to his need (≈ give in to his feelings, say the word). Then his control *does* break after she comes: he takes her urgently, human and helpless (not rough-dark — a man out of the strength to wait), her **second orgasm off his loss of control**, her passion tipping him over. The withholding **relocates from his body to the word**: he loses control of everything *but* the name. Coda: he sleeps; she lies in the cooling sweat and his spend, *marked all over*, and the want of the name finds her — **she belongs to him; does he belong to her?** His body said yes, helplessly, and it still isn't the name. She has no name for the best thing in her life — not the big one he won't give (the word) nor the small one she can't find (*boyfriend*; what she'd tell her mother, how she'd lay him out for her father). Nothing resolved; the proxy dissolved, the real thing buried deeper under a warm true thing.

## The knot (reread-only)

His honest refutation of *just sex* is true (his care is genuine) **and** the whole relationship is instrument (the orchestration). She argues herself out of the true thing using true evidence — refutes the truth with the truth. The book's central *real-AND-instrumental* engine, dramatized in a single fight. Earn the light: the bedroom warmth, the wit as armor, the genuine makeup are all real on first read; the cold lives only in the pattern and the coda.
