# Pitch — With a Long Spoon

*External-facing hook material (jacket / query / positioning). Psychological literary erotica; comps: Gaitskill, Duras (*The Lover*), Salter (*A Sport and a Pastime*), *Story of O*.*

---

## Logline

A man whose genius is *attention* — for seeing the one thing you don't dare admit you want, and giving it to you — draws two women into a bargain in which the tenderest love and the deepest harm are the same act. Everyone consents. No one comes out whole.

---

## Elevator pitch

*With a Long Spoon* opens with a soft-spoken craftsman finishing something he's been building for seven months — a bench — and using it, unhurried, to take a woman raised never to need anyone and make her beg in a voice she hasn't used since childhood. Then he calls her beautiful. That's the whole engine in one scene: his attention is so exact it reaches the thing you've spent your life hiding, and *reaching it is the wound*. The seeing and the wounding are one act; the depth of the love is the depth of the damage.

Then that attention turns on a second woman. One bright afternoon the man and his first lover half-play a game — pointing down a campus walk, "shopping" for a third — and land on a redhead laughing with her whole body, a girl so unguarded the woman doing the grading finds nothing in her to grade. It's the lover who gets sent in: a collision on the quad staged so cleanly the redhead apologizes, a name traded, a friendship begun — and, in its own time, an introduction to the man she was picked for. She never knows she was chosen before they met. Two of the three do.

From there, two seductions run in parallel and never touch: he beckons her forward with his body; her glamorous new best friend produces with words what he produces with his hands. Both arrive at the same place. It's a book about **consent as the foundation of freedom, not its enemy** — and the one hole in that architecture: it guards against *force*, and never against *being deceived into a real yes*. Everyone gets exactly what their plan called for and loses the thing the plan was secretly for. It ends on a blindfolded threesome where the one woman who literally cannot see becomes the only one who finally does.

## Back-cover copy

> Vivienne Thorne has spent her life on good enough — fine men, fine friendships, pleasant-enough pleasures — because good enough was all that was ever handed to her. She's hungry in a way she's never had a word for. Then two people arrive who change the scale of everything: a lover whose whole gift is attention — who sees her at a resolution no one else offers, asks before he takes and takes only what she gives, and draws out of her wanting she'd have been ashamed to name; and a glamorous new friend who seems to understand her better than anyone ever has. She has never been so awake, or so happy. What she cannot see is that the two of them knew each other first — that she was chosen, and steered, guided toward the same door from opposite sides. In the tradition of Gaitskill and Duras, *With a Long Spoon* is a novel of appetite, shame, and ownership, and a question with no comfortable answer: what is a yes worth when you were built to give it? Consent guards against force. It has never once guarded against being loved into the thing you would have refused.
