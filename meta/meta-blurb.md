# Blurb / jacket copy

External reader-facing copy for *With a Long Spoon* — marketing and positioning only, not part of the writing process. Two registers throughout: **explicit** (names the threesome plainly; self-selects the right reader) and **implication** (holds the literary tone and a thread of unease; lets the reader infer). Suggested deployment: explicit on the medium (retail workhorse), implication on the long jacket (where tone matters most). Genre and craft discipline at the bottom.

---

## Explicit

### Short (~55 words)

> Randi has everything: the looks, the poise, and a lover no one knows about. What she wants now is a third in their bed — and she's chosen Vivienne. Vivienne doesn't know any of this. She only knows that two people have walked into her life and made her feel, for the first time, fully alive.

### Medium (~120 words)

> Miranda — sleek, contained, certain of everything — picked her out across the quad: the girl with the curly red hair who laughed with her whole body. *That one.* Her lover agreed. What they wanted was a third, and neither of them told Vivienne she'd been chosen.
>
> What Vivienne knows is this: a brilliant, watchful graduate student has begun teaching her, in private, how much her own body wants. And the most magnetic girl on campus has chosen her as a confidante, drawing her secrets out across long Sunday lunches. Two intimacies, deepening in parallel through one academic year, each remaking her by degrees — and converging, by design, on a single bed.
>
> The reader sees the shape of it from the first page. Vivienne never does. *With a Long Spoon* is a novel about consent, shame, and the cost of being fully alive.

### Long / jacket (~180 words)

> They chose her before she ever knew there was a choice. Pace and Randi — a young mathematician who lives alone at the end of a long drive, and the poised, frictionless girl who is secretly his — have decided, between them, that Vivienne Thorne will be the third in their bed. Vivienne knows none of it.
>
> What she knows is that her junior year has cracked open. Pace acts where other men ask, and watches her with an attention that feels like being read down to the floor. Randi, frank and dazzling, makes a project of her — drawing out over brunch the shames Vivienne was raised to bury, and naming them back to her as gifts. One seduction is hidden and erotic; the other is spoken aloud across a table. Both are steering her, step by deliberate step, toward a single night the other two have been planning all along.
>
> Written in the lineage of Gaitskill, Duras, and Salter, *With a Long Spoon* hands the reader the whole architecture on the first page and then makes them love it anyway — a year-long seduction, rendered at full heat, that asks what we are willing to become in order to feel, at last, completely seen.

## Genre

Psychological literary erotica — literary erotica *with structural-argument ambition*, where interiority and the underlying argument carry the load plot mechanics carry in genre. Comp shelf: Mary Gaitskill, Marguerite Duras's *The Lover*, James Salter's *A Sport and a Pastime*, *Story of O*.

## Craft discipline (obey in every blurb)

- **Foreground the configuration; protect the landing.** The plan — Randi and Pace are lovers, Randi selects Vee as their third — is open from chapter one. The book runs on dramatic irony (dread, not mystery); the reader stands ahead of Vee. Naming the configuration is the *hook*, not a spoiler. What recontextualizes is the *ending* — the inversion of who sees, the fused good/harm verdict, the orphaned wound. Never disclose how it lands.
- **End on the tightening, not the consequence.** Close on the deepening/exposure; never on catastrophe, the threesome's aftermath, or who is destroyed.
- **Never name the appetite.** Show aliveness (the girl who laughs with her whole body); don't explain it. The contained-girl-points-at-the-unguarded-one image carries the thesis silently. Naming it kills it — same earn-the-dark-by-being-light rule as the prose.
- **Pace is older in bearing, not in years** (≈22–23 to Randi's 21). Render him as out of phase with campus — a math PhD candidate who started college at fifteen, living alone off the highway — never as a May-December "older lover."
