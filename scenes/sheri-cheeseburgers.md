# Cheeseburgers

He made the decision before the door had swung shut behind him: he would not bring her down today. Not this week, not this time of year. Whatever he'd carried in from the truck could keep; he had long practice at deciding a thing could keep. And then he was only here, in the warm griddle-smelling dark, and it was easier than it had any right to be.

The place was a double-wide that had stopped being a trailer sometime around the Carter administration and become, by long consent, a diner — out on the county road, where a business lived only by being the sole one for eleven miles. White siding, a tin roof painted and repainted against the rust. Inside, the griddle hissed and the jukebox stood dark in its corner, and he was grateful for that silence like a man come out of a crowded room into an empty one. Every business in three counties had been piping the same dozen Christmas songs since before Thanksgiving. Here there was only the griddle, and a fork somewhere on a plate, and Sheri, shrugging out of her coat by the back booth.

She was a small thing, five feet of her and every inch in motion even standing still — a round face gone a little sallow under makeup — too much of it, slightly off, and worn like she'd dare you to mention it. Her hair was two colors, bleached pale and grown dark at the root, permed at the ends where it fell over the collar of a blue chambray shirt. Good jeans, boots that had seen real winters. A country girl on a cold day, making it work on her own dime.

He waited for her to slide in first, then took the bench across.

Under the glass of the tabletop, laid down years ago and gone amber at the edges, a young Elvis kept his jaw still narrow, a family of four in matching Sunday wool laughed at something past the frame, and a car that hadn't been built in his lifetime or his father's turned on a slow turntable under studio lights. He thought, before he could stop it, that Vee would either love this place or never see it at all, and he wanted very badly to know which.

Vee had tried once to take him to the other kind — the Chili's out by 460, screens on four walls playing three different games, a music track coming down through the ceiling on top of all of it — and something in him had come apart very quietly over the hour, the evening sliding out from under him as his smile got tighter, every sound in the room arguing with him at once — the screens, the music, the crowd. Her voice was down under all of it. He couldn't pull it clear, however hard he tried. Here he could have heard her whisper. He wondered whether she'd let him.

The waitress came over — a woman in her forties, dark hair framing a tired face that had kept its smile, a diner tee and a half-apron over her jeans. "The usual?" she said, already writing, because there was no question and never had been, and they told her yes like people saying yes to their own names.

While the griddle worked they talked about nothing that cost anything. He'd been to California in November for a conference — Sheri had never been west of Knoxville and wanted the whole of it; Berkeley, he said, was a made-up place, people who agreed about everything and dressed like they didn't. She laughed at that. School was thinner this term, part-time now that there was nobody behind her writing checks — she said it plainly and moved on before he could feel anything about it on her behalf.

"Brooks and Dunn cover band's coming to the hall," she said. "First Saturday in February. You're coming."

"Am I."

"You're coming, and you're dancing, and I don't care what the redhead's got to say about it."

The food came out with a kid who didn't know them. He set the double and the chocolate shake down in front of Pace, and the single in front of Sheri — all five feet of her — with the grave confidence of arithmetic, and was gone before anyone could set him right. Sheri scowled at the single. Pace chuckled, and she slid the plates back to true and drew the shake to her by its base like something owed.

Sheri ate like she rode, all of her folded down into it, no ceremony and no apology, a third of the way into the double before he'd so much as picked his up. He was in no hurry. Everyone he knew wanted something off him — to be wanted, or to have a problem solved — and he paid it out without ever letting the cost show. Sheri wanted none of it. She didn't want him, she wasn't impressed by him, and she'd say so to his face; she just liked him and gave him hell, and that was all. He'd come in tonight needing it more than he'd have said.

Behind him a coin dropped into the jukebox, and the machine woke in its corner, and the quiet he'd been grateful for was over. Elvis, thin and young and a long time gone, starting in about being home for Christmas.

Sheri didn't look up from her plate. "Somebody always plays it," she said, and went on eating.

He let it play. There was nothing to say over it that wouldn't press the bruise, so they didn't. He waited until she was most of the way through the double, then asked it the other way. "How are things, though." She knew which things he meant.

"Things are things." She talked around a mouthful. "Cold. Slow. Shop's dead till the students come back."

That wasn't what he'd asked and she knew it. He picked up his own burger and waited. The kaiser gave under his teeth; the pickle came through sharp, then the cold crunch of the lettuce, then the mustard, and it was a good plain thing. His tea had left its rings down the inside of the glass where he'd drunk it down. Behind her the window had fogged over, warm on their side, cold and dark on the other, the whole county road lost out past the breath of the room. He was in no more hurry with the question than with the food.

She held out about as long as he did. "There was somebody," she said. "Paige. Junior in the design school — made these little prints, birds mostly. Had a laugh way too big for her." She almost smiled. "Came from a house that's seven at the table, everybody hollering over everybody. I liked it there. Liked her." Then, flat: "Lasted a couple months."

"What happened."

"What always happens." The shrug had a burr in it. "Couldn't get right with me not talking to my people. Kept picking at it. Like it's something I'd fix if I tried a little harder — call your mama, Sheri, it's Christmas." She did the sing-song and then killed it flat. "Ironic. Isn't it."

He didn't touch the irony. It sat on the table between them and they both saw the grain of it, and there was nothing clever he could lay over it that wouldn't insult what it was.

"People who've never lost anything think grief is a choice," he said.

The song had run out somewhere back, and nobody had fed the machine again. Sheri looked at him a beat longer than she needed to. "Don't waste the good stuff on me." She dragged the shake back over. "Save it for your redhead. How is she — the curvy one." She grinned. "Hell, that backside. I got maybe four seconds of her at the bar before she started throwing things, and I still think about it more than's decent."

He didn't answer right off.

"Huh." She watched him. "What, you had your fill of that hot body?"

"No."

"She find out about the brunette, then."

"No. Not like that."

"Then what is it."

He weighed it. "She wants a declaration." And then, plainer: "She wants me to say it."

"Is that so hard?"

He didn't answer that. He answered the one next to it, the one he had answers to. "I'm there. Every weekend, I'm there — the weeknights were hers, she wanted them for her school and I gave her that. When I've got her, there's nothing else in the room." He could hear himself laying it up brick on brick and couldn't stop, because every brick was true and the true things were supposed to come to something. "I told her she was the most important thing in my life. I said it, out loud, in words. And she looked at me like I'd handed her half of something."

"Don't bullshit me, Pace." She wasn't smiling now. "Most men'd just lie — tell her whatever gets them through the night. Easier that way."

"I'm not them."

"I know you're not." No praise in it — a flat fact, the kind she'd have stated about the weather. "But that's a hell of a lot of reasons for a man who's already got the girl. She didn't ask you for reasons." She set the shake down. "What is it you want."

"I want Vee. I miss her."

"She's told you what you have to do." She leaned in on her forearms, small and level across the amber light. "So say it."

"I can't."

"Then not saying it matters more to you than having her." She held his eyes and didn't let go of them, didn't soften a thing. "And I'll tell you — from this seat, that feels pretty shitty."

He didn't argue. There was nothing in it to argue with. Outside, the dark had come all the way down against the window, and for a moment the griddle was the only sound in the place.

She reached over, stole a fistful of his fries, and ate them.

"Anyhow." She waved a fry, closing the folder. "That brunette you brought dancing before Thanksgiving — the sorority one. Easy on the eye, I'll give her that." A pull at the straw. "Bet she's a lot, though."

"You met her?"

"Y'all came in dancing, I hugged you hello. She looked at me like I was one more of the girls who wanted what she had." A beat. "Never did learn my name. But I got to see her."

He didn't answer that, and she dropped it.

"See, here's what I don't get." The fry pointed at him. "Two years I've known you, and you've never once been a hit-'em-and-quit-'em man. But I have never seen you gone on a girl like you're gone on this redhead."

"Her name is Vee."

"Yeah. The one with the big tits." Around the straw, and pleased with herself for saying it. "So why. What's she got."

He turned the glass, looking for the true shape of it. "The air's different when she's in the room. It — crackles."

Sheri looked at him flat. "You mean the sex is good."

"That's not what I meant." He held it. "But, yes. It is."

She cackled, a real one, loud enough the waitress looked over. "Bet those long legs feel like Christmas wrapped around you."

"Sheri."

"What. Tell me they don't."

His face was hot and he knew it, and he went on anyway. "She sees things nobody else does. I'll cook her something and she won't just tell me it tastes good — she gets what it's for. The whole idea under it. The philosophy of it."

Sheri set her burger down and stared at him. "The philosophy of your food." She shook her head. "Lord almighty. Get over yourself."

And he laughed — the first full one of the night — and she grinned at him across the wreckage of two cheeseburgers, and for a while the thing he couldn't do sat further off, and the diner was warm, and the fog on the glass kept the cold where it belonged.
