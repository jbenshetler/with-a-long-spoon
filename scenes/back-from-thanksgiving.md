# Back from Thanksgiving — Tells Cassie

She found Cassie in the common room with an open suitcase on the floor and a sweater she was refolding for no clear reason.

"You're back," Cassie said.

"Three hours ago." Vee dropped her bag by the door and took the armchair. Her coat was still on. "How was yours?"

"Fine. Loud. My aunt brought her boyfriend." Cassie set the sweater aside. "How was yours?"

"Fine." She thought about it. "Dad burned the rolls again."

"He does that on purpose."

"I think so too."

They sat with that for a moment — the particular ease of people who've shared a wall for two years. Outside, someone was dragging a suitcase over the concrete walkway.

"Did your mom do the thing?" Cassie asked.

Vee looked up. "What thing."

"The thing. The questions."

She had been waiting for this. She'd thought about it on the bus, the three-hour dark ride back through the mountains, and she still didn't have a good answer. "She asked if I was seeing anyone."

Cassie waited.

"I said it was hard to explain."

"Is it?" Cassie's voice was careful — not prying, just exact. "Or do you just not want to explain it?"

Vee looked at her coat zipper. The pull tab was a small silver rectangle. She had nothing that fit in Cassie's frame — not the right word for what he was, not the right story for how it had gone, not the version of herself that stood up cleanly in the telling. She was aware, dimly, that this was information.

"Both," she said. "Probably both."

Cassie nodded and picked up the sweater again. She had a particular gift for knowing when a door was closed. Vee had always been grateful for it.

Right now she was also, a little, not.
