# Back from Thanksgiving — Tells Cassie

The athletic cafeteria had the better food and, more to the point, somewhere to actually sit, so that was where they ended up, the two of them, neither an athlete, at a table near the windows. Sunday dinner, the night everyone came back. A low murmur under the high ceiling, syncopated by the clink of steel on hard plastic.

"How was it," Vee asked. "Home."

Cassie thought about it. "Good to see my parents. My sister has the new baby, so it was all baby, the whole time. Her husband's alright. A little bit of a dork." She speared something on her plate. "It was good to leave, too. They still treat me like I'm in high school. Not overtly. But I can tell they don't quite see me as an adult yet." She looked up. "What about you?"

"Good," Vee said, and meant it. "They were so proud. The honor society thing. Mom cried on the phone when I told her, and then she cried again at the table, just remembering it." She smiled down at her tray. "Dad burned the rolls. On purpose, I'm pretty sure."

"He does that."

"He does." Vee turned her fork over. "Mom had the picture. From the induction. She'd printed it out, she had it in her purse, she kept taking it out to show people who'd already seen it. I cropped it before I sent it. The version they have stops at the waist."

Cassie waited.

"And Dad kept looking at it. Not at me, at the dress." She set the fork down. "And Mom had thoughts about the top of it. The shoulders. She didn't say anything, just touched her own collarbone while she looked. You know the one."

"I know the one," Cassie said.

"And then of course it turned into, well, who took you to this thing. Who gives a girl a dress like that. Who is he." Vee heard the next part before she said it. "I told them he was my boyfriend. I think I put a question mark on it. Out loud. Like I was asking them."

Cassie didn't laugh, which was the right call.

"I told Dad the parts that fit." Vee looked at the window, the dark already down over the parking lot. "He's a math PhD candidate. We went hiking. He taught me to dance on a square of concrete with a boom box before he ever took me to a hall. Dad was happy. More than happy. A boy doing a doctorate who does the right things with his daughter, that's something a father gets to be proud of."

She picked at the edge of her tray.

"And then he looked at the dress again and went quiet. He was doing the math on what it cost. An expensive gift from a man means his daughter is in deeper than he'd pictured. And he pictured someone older than Pace really is. To Dad, a math PhD is a man with grey coming in, not someone barely older than me."

"I didn't tell him Pace cooks for me. Or that he owns his house. A man who cooks for you and owns his house, my father would really start looking for the catch. Too good is its own kind of suspicious."

"And what I couldn't tell him at all is that Pace made the dress. That he sewed it himself, on a machine on his sun porch, because I mentioned once I'd seen one in a magazine I couldn't afford." She said it slowly, like she was finding the size of it. "Honestly, my dad would take it better if I told him Pace takes that dress off me than that he made it. Sex he understands. The sewing he never would."

That pulled a short laugh out of Cassie.

"And it wouldn't even be that he'd think it was wrong. He'd think it was weird. In some ways weird is worse than wrong. Wrong is something he understands and rejects. Weird is something that doesn't fit anywhere in how he sees the world." She stopped. "And the strange thing is, I'm not ashamed of it. The opposite, if anything."

Cassie had stopped eating. "So what is he," she said. Careful, exact, not prying. "Not to your dad. To you."

Vee looked at her tray. She had nothing that fit in Cassie's frame, not the right word for what he was, not the right story for how it had gone, not the version of herself that stood up cleanly in the telling.

"Is it that you can't say," Cassie asked, "or that you don't want to?"

"Both," Vee said. "Probably both."

Cassie nodded and went back to her plate. She had a gift for knowing when a door was closed, and she didn't push at it. Vee was grateful. She was also, a little, not.
