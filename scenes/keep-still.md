# Keep Still

He'd told her to come see the gym sideways, tossed off, as if it were nothing, as if she could take it or leave it. She had not been able to leave it. He had a place he went four mornings a week and a fifth when the week had been bad, a place that made him into whatever he was when she wasn't watching, and he had never once offered to show it to her, and now he had, and she'd said yes before he finished the sentence.

It was up a flight of concrete stairs over a tire shop, and it did not look like the campus rec center where she'd gone twice a semester with good intentions and a full water bottle. The carpet had been a color once. The mirrors were spotted black at the corners. Along the far wall the dumbbells climbed in a rack from small ordinary weights up and up to black iron things the size of engine parts, heavier in one hand than she was in all of herself, so big they read as a joke someone had left lying out, decoration, nothing a body could really pick up. And then a man picked one up.

There was no music. The absence of it sat in the room like a missing tooth the tongue keeps going back to. No sound system, no front-desk playlist laid over the top of it; just the room's own noise, the grunt and blow of men working, the flat clash of iron setting down on iron, a plate knocking a plate. Everyone who wasn't lifting had a cord running up into his ears, sealed off, each man alone inside his own head in a room full of the same labor. Pace didn't wear them. Pace, she was learning, did not seal himself off from a room. He read it.

They stretched first. He showed her without making a lesson of it, folding down over one leg and then the other, and she followed him down and did what he did a beat behind, a copy, and the copying was its own small pleasure, being led through his morning in his place, learning the shape of a thing he knew in his body and she didn't.

Then the bench. He set her under the bar and it was nothing, the empty bar and two of the small plates, a weight she could have carried across the room in her arms. He set her under it anyway as though it mattered. He moved her hands out along the knurling and closed her fingers where he wanted them and pressed once on each to seat the grip, and walked her feet back flat under her knees, and set her shoulders down into the bench with the flat of his hand between them, and he did all of it with the same slow attention he'd have given a man about to press twice his own weight. That was the thing that got into her first. Not the weight. That he took her weight seriously. That the smallness of what she was doing bought her back none of his care.

Then he stood in at the head of the bench to spot her, and she was on her back looking up, and he was over her in nothing but the shorts, standing in close above her head with his hands loose over the bar, and she remembered.

She knew this about him. It was a settled fact of him, filed months ago on a red-shorts afternoon with a shout of triumph in it. But she had never had the fact delivered from underneath, from flat on her back with him standing in over her face, and it arrived new. Only she knew. In a whole room of men not one of them knew what she knew, that he had come in here with nothing on under the thin cloth and was standing over her exactly like that, and the knowing went down through her warm and private and entirely wrong for a public bench.

She pressed the bar up and it went fine, once, twice. On the third he leaned in to spot her as he'd spot anyone at the top of a set, close, his hands riding the path the bar would take if it fell, and the backs of his knuckles came against her breasts because that was where the bar was and that was where his hands had to be, and there was nothing in his face that meant anything by it. He was working. And she, undone by his knuckles and his shorts and the whole upside-down fact of him, lost it, the entire thread of it, and started to laugh, and the bar wandered, and he took it off her clean in one motion and set it in the hooks and looked down at her laughing with one eyebrow up.

"Something funny," he said.

"No," she said, which was a lie, and he knew it was a lie, and let her keep it.

He lay down and pressed himself then, and he kept it light the first two sets, and she understood why without being told. She couldn't have saved him if it went wrong. She couldn't have lifted off him what she couldn't lift, so he stayed inside what he could put back down on his own. Even that was a courtesy to her. He was arranging the morning so that she never once had to be responsible for what she couldn't hold.

By the third set he had loaded the bar. He built it up in plates, sliding them on and collaring them each, and the bar sank and bowed a little across the hooks under the weight, three of the big plates a side, and he lay down beneath it and set his hands and breathed, and pressed it up off the hooks, and brought it down to his chest, and drove it back up.

That was more than she weighed. The bar bowed under it, more than he weighed too, she thought, stupidly, and on the sixth and last press he drove it up on one hard whoosh of breath, working, controlled, nowhere near the end of himself: more than the both of them together. He racked it and sat up and wiped his face on his shoulder, hardly winded, and she looked at him and could not have said one word.

They moved to the cages along the back wall, two of them side by side, steel uprights taller than either of them with the safety pins set through at the height of a squat. Again he stretched, the legs this time, the muscles they were about to use, and again she went down after him.

He went first. He loaded a couple of the plates and stepped under the bar and stood it off the rack onto his back and stepped clear, and squatted, counting under his breath so quietly she caught it only because she was close, down and up, down and up, unhurried, twenty of them in a long even file. She had seen him with nothing on more times than she could count. But she had seen him in bed and she had seen him at rest, and she had never seen his body doing this, working, under the bright ceiling light with a sheen coming up on him, the big muscle of his thighs bunching and letting go and bunching again, each one, it seemed to her and near enough to true, thicker through than her own waist. His thighs worked and she forgot to be anywhere else. It was not a thing she chose. It happened to her on its own, complete, and pulled her attention down to nothing but him.

When he was done he stripped the bar down, carried the big plates two at a time to the low pegs and hung them, and then took two of the small ones, the ones that had made her arms feel foolish on the bench, and slid them onto her bar, and against the memory of his they looked so slight on the long steel that they might have been hung there to be looked at rather than lifted.

In the cage beside them two young men had come in, students by the loose easy loudness of them, and set up to squat. They came in at the edge of things, two more loud bodies in a loud room, and then she caught Pace looking at their bar. One flat look, and something in his face closed and then opened again and let it go. She followed the look and saw a loaded barbell and two boys laughing about something, and could not have said what he'd seen. The ends of their bar were bare. It meant nothing to her. She turned back to her own.

He set her under the bar as he'd set her on the bench, exactly, no faster for the weight being nothing. He moved her in until the steel lay across the meat of her upper back and shoulders, lower than felt right to her, so that she said so, and he said that was where it went, and put her hands on the bar out wide and told her how to take it, and she stood it up off the pins. It was heavier than the little plates had promised. It settled and found its seat across her shoulders and pressed down, and she stepped back out of the rack with it and shifted the load foot to foot as he told her, learning where it wanted to sit.

He stood in behind her, close, his hands flat on her back, on the broad muscle under her arms, and took her down through it with his own body, slow.

"Deeper," he said, low, at her ear. "Don't bounce it. Control it down and then it's yours to send back up."

She went down into it with his hands reading her, and up, and it was hard and it was good and she was concentrating so hard on the man and the weight and the low voice at her ear that she nearly didn't hear the other cage at all.

Nearly. She caught the shape of it a half second before it went wrong, a glass already off the edge of the table with no time in the seeing to do anything but see it fall. The boys had started their set. One was down under his bar and the other said something to him with a laugh in it, *you want it in the butt,* and there was the smack of a hand, and the one squatting jerked and laughed and came up crooked, and his bar tipped. A plate came off the low end of it away from her and hit the floor with a flat enormous bang, and the bar with the weight gone off one side pitched the other way and threw its plate loose toward her, and it came down and rolled, iron over that flat old carpet, into her cage, to her feet.

She flinched. That was all it took. Under the bar with the weight seated across her shoulders she jerked away from the thing coming at her, twisted from it sideways and back all at once, and something in her neck caught and tore its small tear, a wrongness that went white and hot up into her skull, and a sound came out of her she hadn't decided to make.

And then his hands were on her, around her, one flat across her chest and one at her shoulder, and he had her, and he stood her up straight and walked her the two steps back into the rack and set the bar down into the pins in one long controlled motion so that the weight went off her and onto the steel and she was out from under it, empty-shouldered, held.

"Easy," he said. "I've got you. Let it go."

He got the bar settled and got her turned and walked her out of the cage with an arm around her and sat her on a flat bench a little way off and crouched down in front of her. He looked at her, at her eyes and then at her hand where it had come up on its own to the side of her neck. He put his hand over hers there a moment, light. He didn't ask if she was all right. He looked at her neck under her hand, and a decision moved across his face.

Then he stood and turned around and put himself between her and the other cage, and the size of him changed without his moving at all.

He didn't shout. His voice went the other way, down and quiet and very exact, and it carried the space between the cages without being lifted at all.

"You hurt her," he said. "Your carelessness hurt her."

The two of them had gone still. The one who'd been squatting came toward Pace with his hands already up, sorry, man, we're sorry, we didn't see her, and Pace let him get that far and no further.

"Apologize to the woman you hurt," he said.

They turned to her then, both of them, and the one who'd done the smacking looked younger than he had a minute before and a good deal less sure, and they were sorry in the plain scared way of people who have just understood that a thing they did for a laugh had a body on the far end of it. Was she okay. Was she hurt. Did she need anything. They asked it over the top of each other.

She said she was fine. She didn't fully understand what had happened to her, only that a weight had come at her out of nowhere and she'd twisted off it and there was a hot wire in her neck now, and against the size of their fright and Pace's cold quiet and the men beginning to glance over, she wanted only for it to be finished, for all of it to stop pointing at her. It's okay, she said. I'm okay. It was an accident. She meant it, and it reached them, and they sagged with the relief of being let off, and said it once more, sorry, to her, and meant that too, and went back to their cage and took up the collars they hadn't bothered with and put them on, chastened, quiet, and after that the room went back to being only iron and breath.

He heard her. Whatever he'd been holding stayed in him a moment past her words, a beat behind them, and then went out of his shoulders and was gone.

He came and crouched in front of her again and looked at her neck and didn't like it and didn't say so. Then he went and stripped her bar, her two slight foolish plates, and carried them to the pegs and hung them where they went, square and quiet, everything put back where it belonged, and he picked up his shake and her jacket and the towel.

"I'm fine," she said. "Go on and finish. I've wrecked enough of your morning."

He didn't answer the offer in it. He didn't tell her she was hurt. He didn't argue with her at all. He settled her jacket over her shoulders and picked up the bag.

"We're done," he said, easy, about the workout and not about her, and it was decided without ever having been a discussion.

And sitting there with her hand still at her neck, the truth her mouth had gotten out ahead of began to arrive: the muscle along the side of her neck stiffening and setting now that the fright was draining out of her, tightening by the minute into a thing she was going to have to own up to, so that by the time she stood to follow him out she already knew, as surely as she'd once known about the long way she still had to fall, that she was not fine, and that he had known it before she did.
