They rode Saturdays when the weather let them, out past his place. The road rolled, and they talked where it let them, on the flats and the long descents, and went quiet on the climbs, which took the breath for themselves.

Sheri rode on his left, close enough that he could have put a hand on her bars. She was small on the bike and fast on it, all of her folded down into the work, and she had been faster than him on the climbs for as long as he'd known her and never once let it go unmentioned.

The cold sat in his fingers. Their breath went back over their shoulders.

"After you left Thursday," Sheri said, "I kept an eye on her."

He kept his eyes on the road and waited.

"All she did was sit by herself, glaring at the floor, nursing the one drink. Danced with nobody." A few slow cranks. "Left alone." Then: "Hell of a backside on her, though. I'll give her that."

It was the same thing he'd have said, if he were the kind to say it. Sheri was the kind. She remarked on women the way other people remarked on the weather, and she'd been doing it across this handlebar for two years, and it had never once cost him anything.

"Vee," he said.

"Vee." She tried the shape of it. "That short for something, or just the letter?"

"Vivienne." And then he was past the name before he'd decided to be, telling her how the redhead had stopped in front of the walnut chair and run her thumb down the grain where the arm met the leg, the joint he'd spent a weekend on, and asked him how he'd gotten it to do that. How she'd pulled one of the math books off the shelf and not put it back like it had burned her. He heard himself going and didn't stop.

When he ran down Sheri was looking at him sideways, the look she got when she'd caught him out.

"Listen to you," she said.

He didn't know what there was to listen to. He said nothing.

The road tilted up. Sheri put her head down and pulled away from him the way she always did, and he watched her go. Her bike was a small frame, not much bigger than a child's, and there was never as much of her as the work she got out of it: the whole small engine of her standing on the pedals and driving the grade, the bleached length of her ponytail swinging across her back with each pump of her legs. He marvelled at it the way he always did, and saved his breath for the hill, and came up at the top a length behind.

She was waiting there, soft-pedaling, not as winded as would have suited either of them.

"You want me to not like her?" she said. She'd do it, too. She could turn it on a person inside a breath and keep it burning for years; he'd seen her do it, and seen what it was worth on the other side, which was everything.

"No," he said. "She couldn't take your heat."

Sheri laughed, pleased, and let it go.

"What happened to the pale sorority chick?"

"That's not finished."

"Huh." She filed it somewhere. The road went by.

"Two of them at once." She shook her head, not at him, just running the arithmetic anybody could run. "If that's not already complicated, it will be."

He didn't answer. Sheri wasn't waiting for one; she'd said her piece and was already looking down the road. He caught her up, and they rode on side by side, the cold going out of the morning, and that was the whole of it.
