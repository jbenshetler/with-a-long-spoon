# The New Ordinary

*Vee POV. A Friday night in mid-November, after the longest stretch of nights apart.*

---

The Corolla had barely stopped before she had the bag up off the seat and the door open into the cold. It rode light in her hand. Half of what she used to drag back and forth lived in the top drawer of his bathroom now and never had to be packed at all, the brush and the cream and the form of green scrunchies and the loose pins, a whole drawer of her set down in a clean man's house, and the bag had gone light around the absence of it. Her space. She smiled at the phrase crossing the dark grass, the private foolish pleasure of it, hers.

It had been five nights, and her own doing, which was the part that still sat wrong in her. She'd been the one to call it. Too many weeks of coming back to the dorm on four hours and somebody else's shower, of opening a textbook and reading the same paragraph four times without any of it going in, of the work stacking up behind her while she burned her nights down to the wick out here. She'd finally said it to him, that she needed a few nights for school, that she was falling behind. He'd said of course. That was all of it. Take the time you need, he'd said, I'll be here. He hadn't argued and he hadn't gone cold and he hadn't made her pay a cent for it, and the plain grace of that had cost her more than a fight would have, because some unreasonable corner of her had wanted him to fight, to want the nights badly enough to say so out loud. He never said so. He only opened the door and waited on the far side of it for her to come back through. Five nights. She'd lasted five.

He had the door open before she reached it. He must have heard the car; he always heard the car. No porch light tonight, only the warm of the house at his back and the bulk of him filling the frame in one of the big soft shirts, and she came up the steps and into him without a word. He took her face in both hands, the cold of her cheeks against the warm of his palms, and kissed her there in the doorway with the night still on her and the bag still hanging off her shoulder. Slow. As though there were no hurry to any of it, as though five nights were nothing, as though she hadn't been counting them. She had been counting them. Her body had kept a running tally her mouth would never once have read out, and the first taste of him called the whole of it due at once.

The bag slid off her shoulder somewhere in the hall. She didn't hear it land.

They made the bed, barely. He undressed her with the unhurried hands he brought to everything, no fumble in them, and the patience of it was its own small cruelty tonight, because she was already long past patience, had been past it somewhere around Wednesday. When he laid her back and put his mouth to her a sound came out of her she could do nothing about, too loud and far too soon, the exact sound of a woman who had told herself a careful story about needing time to study and was being shown now, by her own throat, just how true the story had been. He was in no more hurry there than anywhere. He always came around to her first, always, brought her the whole way over before he took a single thing for himself, and tonight he barely had to work at it. She went up fast and broke fast, shamefully fast, her hips off the sheet and her hand fisted in his hair and five nights coming out of her in one long undignified rush, and she lay there after with her face hot, caught out again, her body having just published the number she'd gone to such trouble to keep private.

He came up the length of her, mouth moving as he came, her thigh and the jut of her hip and the soft of her belly and the underside of each breast, and found her mouth, and she pulled him down into her. He went into her easily, all of him, her body still loose and open and humming from the first of it, and she took him without question now, the old bracing habit long gone, nothing left in her that wanted to argue the point. He moved slow even then. He always had a slowness left in him when she had none, and she gave herself up to it, to the long unhurried work of him, until the second one built in her without her chasing it and broke quieter than the first, a deep slow roll she rode out with her face turned into his neck, and felt him let go a moment after, holding still and deep and shaking just slightly, the only time the control ever left him.

Afterward he fed her. He near always did.

She came out in his robe with her hair shoved up anyhow and found him already at the counter, gone down into the work, the place he went when he cooked that she'd learned to simply sit and watch. He turned a round of dough out of the bread machine onto the floured white laminate, dough he'd set going before she ever arrived, and worked it under the heels of his hands until it gave him what he wanted, then pressed and pulled it out flat. On the back burner a pan had been going low and slow since before she'd come, romas he'd skinned and a yellow onion and garlic and a slug of the wine, and he stood over it and brought it down for what had to be half an hour, tasting it off the spoon, adding a little cream near the end and nothing else she could see, until it had gone from a loose red mess to something dark and thick and smooth that he spread over the dough in long even strokes with the back of the spoon.

He grated the cheese over it himself, a pale wet ball of it he unwrapped from paper. "From buffalo," he said, near the only thing he'd said in ten minutes. "The European kind." Slivers of yellow onion laid on in a loose wheel, button mushrooms sliced thin and scattered over. A pinch of cornmeal thrown onto the stone that lived in his oven, a sound like a handful of sand. Then the wooden peel, the flat long paddle of it, and a short practiced snap of his wrist, and the whole thing was off the peel and onto the stone and the door shut on it.

She'd been drinking the wine he'd poured her, a Nebbiolo, he'd said, dark and full of dried cherry and a little of something like tar at the back of it that she'd decided she liked. He had a glass of it too and had barely touched it, a sip when he thought of it, because the cooking had taken him, all of him, the helpless whole of his attention down on it. He wasn't performing any of it for her. That was the part she could have watched all night. He'd have made the pizza exactly like this for an empty house. The care was only the shape his attention took when it came down on a thing, and tonight some of it had come down on her and some on a ball of dough, and she could not have said, watching him, which of them got the larger share, and found she didn't mind.

Fifteen minutes and he drew it back out on the peel and cut it on the board and put a slice in front of her, and she ate it too hot and then stopped with her mouth full and her eyes up.

She could taste all of it. That was the strange thing, the thing she had no frame for. Not one flavor but every flavor in it, separately, laid out for her to walk down one at a time. The yeast in the crust, faintly sour and alive. The bright acid of the tomato and beneath it a sweetness the long cooking had pulled up out of it. A late heat that arrived after the rest and sat on the back of her tongue. The onion still with a crunch to it and a clean sting. The mushroom gone soft and giving, holding the wine. It was nothing like the pizza she knew, the flat orange sameness of that, one taste pretending all on its own to be a meal. This came apart on her tongue into all its separate pieces and let her count them. It was the difference, she thought, between cotton candy and a bowl of cut strawberries, the one a single flat sweet that dissolved and left nothing behind it, the other a dozen small sharp true things arriving at once.

He was watching her when she looked up, pleased, the small private smile, as if her face had just told him something he'd wanted to know. She didn't ask what. She ate the rest of the slice down to the crust and held her plate out for another.

Later, with the plates done and the kitchen gone dark behind them, she knelt over the bag at last to put herself away into the drawer, the cream and the pins, the few things that still traveled. At the bottom, folded into a square, was the shirt.

She'd washed it. That was the thing she'd come out here to do and had nearly lost the nerve for on the drive. It was his flannel, the enormous no-tag one he'd handed her in the rain on the hike and never asked back, the one she'd slept in every single night she wasn't here, swimming in the soft worn bulk of it, the sleeves past her hands. She lifted it out and held it a moment and then turned and put it into his hands before she could think any better of it. "Here."

He took it and looked at it, and something moved across his face. A puzzlement first, and under the puzzlement something she'd put there without meaning to, the start of a hurt, because he thought she was giving it back. He decided not to ask. He was good at not asking things.

"No," she said, too quick. "No. This is still my shirt. You don't get to keep it."

He looked up at her.

"I had to wash it." It came out small and she couldn't help it. She kept her eyes on the shirt and not on him. "It stopped smelling like you. I wore it every night I wasn't here and it went stale, and then it went to nothing, and one day last week it didn't smell like anything at all, so I washed it." The rest of it came fast then, shoved out all at once to be done with it. "I need you to wear it for me. The whole weekend. And give it back to me Sunday so it smells like you again."

For a second he didn't say anything, and she made herself look up. His face had come open, the puzzlement gone and the hurt gone, and in their place the warm slow smile she only ever got a few of, the one that seemed to cost him something, that he didn't hand around. He had heard all of it. He had heard exactly how many of her nights had been spent breathing a dying shirt, and not one part of it embarrassed him on her behalf and not one part of it was going to be turned into a thing she had to defend.

"All weekend," he said. He pulled it on right then, over the shirt he already wore, the big soft flannel settling across the breadth of him where it had been built to sit, and the foolish hot prick of tears came up in her out of nowhere and she blinked them down before they could fall. "You'll have it back Sunday."

"Smelling like you."

"Smelling like me."

She'd carry it back to the dorm Sunday night full of him, and sleep in him through the nights that weren't his, and bring it out next Friday gone faint again to be filled. She didn't say any of that. There was no need to. He'd worn it for her without making her ask twice, and he stood there now wearing two shirts in his own warm kitchen because she'd told him to, and that was the whole of his answer, as it always was.
