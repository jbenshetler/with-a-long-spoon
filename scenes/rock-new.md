# Rock

Behind them the fire worked quietly in the den. In front of them the glass held the whole cold night: the yard gone black, the ridge a darker black laid over it, the stars out hard and close along the top of the line. The porch had no heat of its own. The cold stood on the other side of the glass and leaned in, and the quilt over the two of them and the fire at their backs made the whole of the warmth, and the smallness of it was the point.

She had her feet tucked under his thigh. He had handed her the robe off the back of the couch and taken the quilt down without being asked, and now they were under it, his boxers and her skin and his robe over her shoulders, and the tea gone cool in the mugs on the low table because they had stopped drinking it in order to keep talking.

She noticed she talked more out here. It was not that he pulled it out of her. He asked a small question and then he waited, and the waiting had nothing in it that wanted the answer sooner, so the answer came out longer and truer than she had meant. She would say a thing and look over and find him already there, arrived at where she was going before she finished getting there, and it made her want to keep going.

They had been on food. He had cooked, earlier, before the rest of it, and she had eaten standing at his counter with her fingers and made a sound she was a little embarrassed by, and now she was trying to explain the sound.

"It's not that I was hungry," she said. "It's that I didn't know food did that. Where I'm from, food is a thing that happens on schedule. It's good. My mother's a good cook. But nothing ever surprised me."

"Where's from."

"Ohio." She said it flat, the name of a place with no accent on it. "It's casseroles. It's a gravy and a potato and a meat, and the meat is one of four meats, and you know before you sit down which of the four it's going to be from what day it is."

He smiled at that, small and real, into the dark of the glass.

"My mother had a spice rack," she said. "A wedding present. It came with the little jars already in it, and it hung by the stove my whole childhood, and I think three of the jars ever got replaced. Pepper. Bay leaf. Cinnamon at Christmas. The rest of them she kept. I found them when I was in high school, looking for something, and half the paper seals were still on. The little foil seals, under the caps. Twenty years and they'd never been opened."

She had not thought about the seals in a long time. She was surprised, saying it, to feel something move under it, a small grief she had no name for and did not chase.

"That's a whole shelf of things she was given and never used," he said.

"That's my mother." She turned it lighter before it could get heavy. "She's a nurse. She irons pillowcases. Everything in her house has a right place, and the right place is where it has always been."

"And your father."

"He fixes airplanes. Jet engines, the big commercial ones. He'll take a thing apart that weighs more than the car and put it back so it flies people over the ocean, and then he comes home and can't work the remote." She heard the fondness in her own voice and let it stay. "They grew up in the same town. They met in church. My mother still calls me Vivienne."

"Not Vee."

"Never Vee. Vivienne. Like she's setting the table." She pulled her knees up a little under the quilt. "My dad calls me Vee. He only calls me Vivienne when I'm in trouble, so I learned it early. You hear the whole name coming and you already know what you did."

"So the name that means love in one mouth means trouble in the other."

She looked over at him. He had said it plainly, laid it where she could see it, not reaching. "I never put it that way," she said. "But yes."

"Siblings."

"One. A brother. Three years up." She thought about how to say it honestly. "He's in the Army. Not married. We're fine. There was never a fight, we just — there's a lot of air between us and neither of us minds it. I couldn't tell you his phone number. I love him. I don't know him very well."

He nodded, taking it in, and did not tell her that was sad, which she was grateful for, because it was not sad, or it was only the ordinary amount of sad that everything was.

"You," she said. "Your turn. I've been talking."

He was quiet a moment. She had learned that his quiet was not reluctance. It was that he answered the actual question, and it took him a second to find the actual answer.

"My mother stays home," he said. "My father works in a chemical plant."

She waited. Nothing else came. It was not withheld. It was only the size of it, flat and factual, next to the warm sprawl of hers, and she felt the difference and did not remark on it.

"What does she call you," she asked. "Your mother."

Something moved across him that she felt more than saw, a small settling, and the fire behind them shifted at the same moment so the reflection of it trembled once at the edge of the glass.

"My real name is Peter," he said.

He did not say what his mother called him. He took her out of the answer and gave Vee only the name, and the name came with a faint putting-down in it, the tone of a thing set on a shelf on purpose and left there, not hidden, only not carried anymore. Peter. She held it and did not turn it over in front of him. She had the sense, holding it, of a whole person standing just behind the one word, a boy with that name, and she did not go looking. He had handed her the near edge of something large and let her choose how far in to walk, and she stopped at the edge, because he had.

"Peter," she said, once, quietly, to feel how it sat in her own mouth.

"Pace, out there." He tipped his head an inch toward the black glass, toward the world past it. "Peter's older than that."

She let it be. She moved her foot against his leg under the quilt, and he put his hand on her ankle and left it there, warm, and they were quiet, and the stars stood over the ridge.

"How old are you," she said. "Actually. I keep meaning to ask and losing my nerve."

"Twenty-two."

She turned under the quilt to look at him. "You're not."

"I am."

"You're getting a doctorate."

"Yes."

"How." She said it before she could dress it up. "People do well on tests. They don't get handed college at your age."

"I did very well."

"That is the least informative modesty I've ever heard."

"I wasn't trying to be modest."

"That's worse."

His mouth moved, not quite a smile, because he was too literal not to first check whether she meant it, and too used to her by now to think she did. "I took the SAT for a summer program when I was fourteen. It became a longer conversation than anyone planned. Scholarships. A great deal of adult concern arranged around the idea that the opportunity shouldn't be wasted." He gave it in order, one fact after another, plain as directions. "It started college at fifteen. It was the best thing that ever happened to me. I don't say that lightly."

"Leaving home at fifteen." She tried to picture it, a boy that age with a suitcase. "That must have been terrifying."

"Less than staying."

He said it so plainly it took her a moment to hear that there was anything in it.

"I wasn't a good fit where I was," he said.

The sentence was almost comic in its restraint. She waited, watching him over the rim of her mug, and he seemed to decide something, or else the porch allowed another fact to rise.

"In seventh grade I taught myself algebra and trigonometry," he said. "Not because anyone taught me. I wanted to write a graphics program, and there was a kind of picture you could only draw if you understood the math under it, so I learned the math. At the kitchen table, on my own, to get the pictures out of the machine." He said it without any weight on it, one flat fact. "And in math class my teacher had me carry my desk out into the hall and do long-division worksheets by myself, with the door shut."

The words reached her one at a time.

"For how long?"

"The year."

She set the mug down on the low table. Carefully; the carefulness seemed to matter, though she could not have said why. Then she shifted under the quilt until her shoulder came to rest against his arm.

"They took the thing you loved," she said, "and made it the punishment."

He looked down at her. Not startled. More as if she had put her hand on a place he had not offered her and had, by accident, found the right one.

"Yes," he said. Contained, precise, the word set down exactly. "That's approximately what happened."

She wanted to say she was sorry. She did not. Sorry would have asked him to accept comfort for a thing he had not meant to open. She pressed her shoulder once against his arm instead, there and gone.

She thought he might stay there. He did not. He came back up out of it, and when he spoke again his voice had changed, had opened, and she understood that the hallway was not where the story lived for him. It was only the door you went through to get to the part he wanted to tell her.

"And then I got to college," he said, "and it was the opposite. Rooms full of people who thought it was beautiful. Who'd stay up arguing about it. That was the first year of my life I wasn't in the hall."

"Beautiful," she said. "Math."

"You don't believe me."

"I believe you. I just never had anybody make me see it."

He shifted under the quilt so he was turned a little more toward her, and something in him leaned forward, quiet but lit, and she recognized it as the thing that came into him when he was about to give her something he cared about.

"There's a shape," he said, "you can fill with paint but never paint the outside of. You could pour a cup of paint in and fill it to the brim, exactly this much and no more, a number you could write down. And the surface of it, the outside you'd have to cover with a brush, goes on forever. There isn't enough paint in the world for the outside of a thing you just filled with a cup." He watched her get it. "That's true. It's not a trick. Somebody proved it, and it stays proved."

"That's not possible."

"It's completely possible. It's just not what your hands expect." He was smiling now, out at the dark. "There's a coastline problem too. Ask how long a coastline is, and the answer is: how carefully are you measuring. Use a shorter ruler and you catch more of the little bays, and it gets longer. Shorter ruler, longer coast. Keep going and it runs off to infinity. A country's edge is as long as you have patience for. The pictures I wanted to make as a kid were made out of that. Edges inside edges, all the way down, forever."

She was watching him more than listening now, though she was listening. "And that's beautiful to you."

"That's the small stuff." He said it like clearing his throat before the real thing. "Newton wrote down three sentences about how things move. Three. And they're enough for a ball you throw, and for a leaf coming off a tree and turning as it falls, and for the stars going around up there. The same three sentences. The ball and the leaf and the stars are all doing the one thing, and once you can read it you can't unsee that they're the same, that it's all one piece." He looked out at the ridge, at the hard stars over it. "People think it's dry. That's only because they meet it after someone's taken the world out of it. It isn't separate from the world. It's the language the world is written in. Most people walk around inside the poem and never get to read a line of it, and I got to read some. And when I love somebody I want to show them a line. Like playing someone your favorite song. Here. Listen to this. Isn't it —"

He stopped. He had heard himself say *love somebody*, and he did not take it back and he did not underline it. He let it stand where it had fallen.

"I'd never thought of any of it that way," she said. Which was true, and was also not the thing she was feeling, which was larger and had nowhere to go.

"It sounds a little lonely," she said. "The hall, and then rooms where nobody else could follow you."

He considered it. "It didn't feel lonely. Not in the work. Not in the rooms where people wanted to talk about it."

He said it without self-pity, and that made it worse and better at once. She could picture him at fifteen, too young and not young at all, in a room where no one made him carry his desk into the hall.

The fire settled behind them. A little of it trembled at the corner of the glass, a second warmth laid faint over the stars.

"Is that why you lift," she said. She had seen him under the bar once, through the garage door, and the look on his face had not been strain. It had been somewhere else entirely, and she had wondered.

He thought about it. "Partly it started stupid. Fifteen in a dorm full of grown men, and I decided nobody was ever going to be able to pick me up and put me in a hallway again." He let that sit a beat, dry. "But that's not why I kept it." He turned his hand over on her ankle, absently, feeling the bone of it. "When I step up to a squat bar with enough weight on it to bend the one-inch steel like dry spaghetti, there can be nothing in my mind but that moment. No future. No past. Only this lift, this heartbeat. And that is freeing."

She was quiet. She was putting something together, and it came together in her body before it came together as a thought, low and warm and a little unsteady.

"That's how you are with me," she said slowly. "It's the same thing. When we're — you're all the way here. You're not thinking of the clever thing to say next. You're not off somewhere in your own head. I've been with people who were somewhere else the whole time. You're right here. It's the lift. It's the same face."

He did not answer that with words. His hand moved up from her ankle, under the quilt, and settled flat and warm against her hip where she was bare under the robe, and stayed, not going anywhere, only being there, present in the way she had just named, and she felt the accuracy of it move through her.

Her own hand had drifted while they talked. It was on his thigh, above the knee, and now it moved without her deciding it, and found, along the outside of his hip, a place where the skin was not like the rest. A seam of it, old, raised and smooth, longer than her hand. Her fingers went along it before she thought about whether to.

"This?" she said.

His hand came over hers. Not fast, not to move it. He laid his hand on top of hers where it was and pressed it gently down against the old seam and held it there.

"Not tonight," he said.

He did not take her hand away. He kept it there under his, on the thing he wasn't going to tell her about yet, and the not-tonight had no wall in it, only a door with a time on it that wasn't now. She turned her hand over under his so their palms were together over the scar, and left it, and that was the whole of the asking and the whole of the answer.

They stayed like that. The tea had gone fully cold. Past the glass the yard held its black and the ridge held its darker black and the stars did not move that she could see, though he had just told her they were moving, that they were doing the same thing the leaf did, that it was all one piece.

"Peter," she said. Softly. No question in it this time. She was not asking him for anything, and she was not trying it on. She was only saying it, once, into the warm dark, the way you say a word to keep it.

He let it sit. He did not answer it and did not need to. His hand closed around hers over the old scar, slow, and held, and the fire kept its small business behind them, and the cold stayed on its own side of the glass, and neither of them said anything else for a while.
