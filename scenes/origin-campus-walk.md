It was the kind of late-September afternoon that the valley produced only a handful of times a year and never on demand — cool enough that the sun felt like a hand laid flat between the shoulder blades, the air dry and moving. The wind came down off the ridge in long unhurried passes, turning the tops of the maples so the undersides of the leaves showed pale, then letting them settle. The grass was still summer-green, would be green another month, but the dogwoods at the edge of the quad had started to rust at the tips and one sugar maple near the chapel had gone over all at once, a single tree burning orange in a field of things that had not yet decided to change.

They came up the brick walk from the lower lot with her hand in his. The wind came down off the ridge and got at her hair the way it got at everything else on the quad and found almost nothing to move — black and fine and gathered back into a sleek ponytail, a scrunchie at the crown the one allowed flourish, the tail swinging clean when she turned her head and not otherwise; she had decided that morning exactly how much of her the day would be permitted to touch, and bound the rest. The campus laid itself out the way it had been built to — the long colonnade of the old academic row, the white pediments and the red brick, the columns throwing their afternoon shadows in even bars across the path, the whole thing composed two centuries ago by men who wanted the eye to travel exactly this way and be exactly this satisfied. Students moved through it in the loose Wednesday-afternoon current, alone and in pairs and in clusters, unhurried, the ones with nowhere to be drifting and the ones with somewhere to be cutting across the grass against the diagonal. Somebody was throwing a frisbee. Somebody had a speaker going, low, two hundred feet off, the bass arriving a half-second behind the rest.

Pace was entirely present to all of it and entirely inside the thing he had decided to do, and the two were not in tension. This was the pleasure — the day arranged itself around him like a gift he was about to hand to her, the light and the wind and the burning maple, and underneath the day the clean line of what he already knew about her, what her body had told him an hour ago in a register her mouth was still busy correcting. He had three orgasms' worth of evidence and one small flinch and the small recovery after the flinch, and the recovery had told him as much as the flinch had. She wanted this. She could not yet hold that she wanted it. He could give her a shape to hold it in. The day was good for it. He had not planned the maple but he was glad of the maple.

They passed a man alone at one of the quad tables with a tray from the grill — a cheeseburger in waxed paper, a paper boat of fries gone slightly translucent with grease, the smell of it carrying on the wind, beef and salt and hot oil, frank and uncomplicated and good.

"I'm hungry," Randi said.

"Do you want a cheeseburger?"

"No." Easy, automatic, not even a glance back at the tray. "I can't eat that."

She was loose beside him, still warm from the afternoon in his bed, the endorphins not yet spent. She had showered and put her face back on and dressed and walked out the door game for whatever the day was, because four weeks had taught her that the days with him were sometimes hiking and sometimes a play in a garden and sometimes a thing he did not name until they were doing it, and she had liked all of them, and she liked best the ones with an edge. He could feel the readiness in her hand. She thought they were going to do something fun. They were.

A woman crossed the path ahead of them, twenty feet up, headed for the colonnade — petite and built to the proper scale, the scale Randi approved of and maintained, narrow through the shoulder and hip, an oval face, a small straight nose, dark hair in a high sleek ponytail that swung when she moved. Leggings and an oversized quarter-zip in the Greek pastel, a canvas tote with a water bottle clipped to it, box-fresh white trainers that hadn't seen a trail. She walked the walk: chin level, phone in hand but not looked at, the unhurried propriety of a girl who has never once wondered whether a room was glad to have her in it. She could have been Randi's little sister. She could have been Randi — and that was where Pace started, with the type nearest her own, because he expected her to choose inside the grammar she lived in, the labels and the lines and the correct shoe, and a girl like this was that grammar walking.

Pace leaned in close, his mouth at the shell of her ear, and let his breath go warm against it on purpose, the way he knew did things to her, and said, low, "What about her?"

Randi went still.

He watched it work through her in stages. For the first beat she was simply confused, and under the confusion something tightened: he was *pointing*, in daylight, at a girl on the path, the bald social wrongness of it, the exposure of being two people who pointed at a stranger and discussed her. He felt the small recoil of that in her hand, the propriety offended. Second beat, the confusion held — what about her *for what* — and he let it hold, let her hang in it, said nothing, did not help.

Then the third beat, and he felt it arrive in her body before her face changed: the question resolved, the frame snapped into place, and she went hunting up the path after the swinging ponytail. He could not see what her eyes did at that distance — only how long they stayed, the weight and the duration of her attention, whether a woman held her or got let go. That, and the hand in his, and whatever her frame told him; it was enough. He knew already what he was reading her for. Her body had told him in bed that she wanted a woman, and her mouth had spent the hour since insisting it was a thing she'd do *for him*, a girlfriend's generous offer, a threesome she was giving him as a gift — and he knew the offer was the camouflage and the wanting was hers and entirely hers. He let the camouflage stand. It was the wrapper the wanting needed to come out in. Letting her keep it was the kindness; it was, in fact, the whole of what he was doing out here.

"No," Randi said. The propriety was gone out of her voice. Something else had come into it, lower, considering. "Too — " She watched the ponytail reach the shade of the colonnade and turn. "Too expected."

"Mm," he said, neither agreeing nor not, and they kept walking, in no hurry at all.

He did not rush her. There was a great deal of afternoon, and the rushing would have done the opposite of what he wanted — would have made it a task, a thing being driven at, and the whole art of it was that it stay a game, a thing they were doing together because it was fun, her edge and his attention, the kind of afternoon she had learned to expect from him. So he let a hundred yards go by with nothing in it but the walk and the wind and the back of her hand under his thumb, and only when the path opened toward the athletic center did he tip his chin at the next one.

Two of them this time, coming out through the glass doors of the rec center with gym bags, mid-laugh at something, both in the matched athleisure that read like a uniform — high-waisted, cropped, one in lavender and one in a sage that was everywhere that year, the same chunky trainers, the same oversized scrunchies, hair scraped up off damp necks, faces bare and bright and shining a little with the workout. Sorority, both of them, or close, and both built lean and correct, the bodies Randi's standard would pass without a second look. The one in sage had the harder body, a sprinter's build and a sharp little chin, and moved fast and sure, an athlete's economy in it; the lavender one was rounder in the face, softer, slower, trailing a half-step and laughing up at her friend. Their air was the same air all the same: bright, closed, complete in each other, a sealed unit that the world washed past.

"What about them?"

Randi considered the pair the way she might consider a dish she had not ordered. "No." A small shake of the head. "Those two go everywhere together. You'd get both or neither." Then, dry, sideways, testing the game's walls: "Is that a yes or a no for you?"

"I'm not the one being asked," he said.

That pleased her. He saw it please her — the corner of her mouth, the half-beat of recalibration as she understood the rules better than she had a sentence ago: the choosing was hers. He was not choosing a woman. He was watching her learn that she could.

Past the athletic center the path ran along the practice fields and then bent toward the library, and somewhere along there he felt the thing in her change. It came up through her hand and through the side of her he was walking against: a low readiness, a faint tension strung through her frame, the particular coil he knew from the dance floor — a follow gathering herself a half-beat before the lead moves, weighting forward, already leaning into a turn that hasn't been called yet. She was scanning ahead of them now. He could feel her wanting him to point, feeling for the next one before he gave it to her, her body asking *that one? now?* into the space before his hand came up. She would have told him, if he'd asked, that she was being a good sport about his game. She had stopped being a good sport about anything a quarter mile back. She was shopping, and the wanting had her, and the only thing she was still managing was the part of her that would have to pretend, later, that she hadn't been. He gave her three more along that stretch and let her think she was humoring him.

A tall girl alone, walking fast, AirPods in, a structured bag carried in the crook of her forearm rather than worn — pre-law, maybe, or business, dressed a half-decade older than she was in a camel coat too warm for the afternoon. Long, lean, a narrow triangular face that came to a tidy chin, handsome more than pretty, and closed all the way down — eyes forward, mouth set, an air that had no give in it and no room in it, a girl moving through the quad as if it were a corridor between two places that mattered more. "No," Randi said, before he had finished pointing. "She'd be a project." She did not elaborate and he did not ask. Not the closed ones, then. Not the ones who'd have to be gotten through.

A girl on the library steps with a friend, the two of them splitting a single pair of earbuds and bent over one phone, heads almost touching, the intimacy of it complete and sealed and not for anyone. Round-faced, a soft snubbed nose, round wire glasses she kept pushing up with one knuckle, a green knit beanie though it wasn't cold. Small — not Randi's trained smallness but a wrapped-up, drawn-in smallness, a build that folded toward itself, a girl who took up as little room as she could and seemed to like it there. Her air was quiet and turned inward and asked nothing of the path. Randi's look stayed on her — past the quick verdicts she'd been handing down, a real considering, the longest she'd held any of them. "Maybe," Randi said.

He stopped walking. He turned and kissed her — not the ear this time, the mouth, unhurried, and he had not wiped his face since the bed and he knew the moment her own taste reached her because her breath caught against his lips and held. He let the kiss finish itself. When he drew back her eyes were a shade darker and her composure had a seam in it.

"Maybe," he agreed, mild, as if they were discussing the weather, and started them walking again.

She laughed, low in her throat, and the thing she said came out warmer and realer than she meant it to — he heard the seam in it, the place where the performance didn't quite cover the actual heat underneath. "*Oh,*" she said. "You dirty boy." Sex-kitten, the trailing register, a hand light on his arm. It would have read to almost any man as pure play, a girlfriend being game. He let her have it.

The *maybe* had taught him something and he tried to use it: not the closed ones, not the mirrors — try open, try warm, try a woman who took up room and was glad to. So he pointed at the most alive one in sight, a striking girl mid-laugh in a knot of friends, a blazer and a blowout that had survived the wind better than it had any right to, the big unbothered authority of someone who chaired things and ran the room while she did, a wide confident face and a smile she was already wearing before anyone had said anything to earn it. He thought he had it. "God, no," Randi said, fast, and there was real feeling in the *no*. He'd guessed wrong, and the wrongness was instructive: not that kind of open, not warmth that ran the room and knew it. The girl's ease was performed, aimed outward, a thing she did *at* people. Closer, but no.

Not the expected, not the matched pairs, not the closed projects, not the ones who ran the room. He was reading the grain of her, the way you read a board before you cut it, finding which way the figure ran — and he'd thought he knew the figure before they left the house. He liked being shown he was right. He liked, even better, the places he was surprised.

The path delivered them, eventually and without hurry, to the broad doors of the dining cafeteria, where the four-o'clock current thickened — students coming and going through the propped doors, the warm institutional smell of the place breathing out into the cool air, trays and voices, the clatter carrying. They drifted to the low retaining wall a little off from the doors, an unremarkable stretch of brick at exactly the right remove, and he let them slow there as if it were nothing, as if they were only two people who had walked a while and might sit a minute. He had not chosen the bench in advance. He chose it now, the way he chose most things, fast and without seeming to, for the angle it gave on the doors.

And then she came out.

She came out of the dining hall the way weather arrives — all at once and filling the space it entered. Tall, and built on a lavish scale, lush in a way that had nothing curated about it and nothing apologized for — full breasts, a deep waist, hips with real width to them, the kind of ripe hourglass the year's clothes were not cut for and that did not care. And she moved like an athlete despite all of it, or under all of it — a low sure center, a balance in her that nothing tipped, the body plainly trained and plainly past the line where training stays competitive, command and abundance riding in the same frame. Whatever she was wearing, Randi's eye would find nothing in it to grade: a soft drapey thing in no particular color, a denim jacket, a canvas bag slung careless and crosswise, scuffed boots. The surface offered the grammar nothing. The hair was the first thing and then not the thing at all — shoulder-length and loose, a warm dark red that sparked to copper wherever the wind lifted it into the sun, and the wind off the ridge was lifting it the whole time, playing with it, nothing pinned and nothing trying to be. A snub nose, tipped up at the end, and chestnut freckles thrown across it and the tops of her cheeks. She was walking with another girl and she had her head thrown all the way back, laughing — balanced even in that, throat bare to the sky, laughing at something with her whole body the way the polished girls and the matched pairs and the closed camel-coat project would never laugh at anything in public, would never let go of the wheel that completely — laughing like a person who had genuinely forgotten, for the length of the laugh, that she could be seen.

"Her," Pace said, and pointed.

He had his eyes on Randi when he said it, because Randi was the instrument he was reading and the girl by the doors was only the note. So he saw it land. He saw the answer come up out of her before she made it a sentence — saw her stop, fully, the loose afternoon walk arrested mid-step, the half-stride dropping out from under her. And then she did the thing she had not done once all afternoon: she did not look away. Every other woman she had weighed and released, her attention moving on; this one held her where she stood, her eyes staying and staying on the red-haired girl as if pulled, the coil he'd felt in her frame all down the library walk releasing now into pure helpless looking. There was no managing in it. The follow had felt the music and broken frame before the lead called the step.

"Yes," she said. The word came out alone, with nothing around it — the bare arrival of a thing already true before she got to it.

Then she heard herself, and he watched her reach for the costume — watched the recovery start its work, her eyes coming back off the girl at last, the warmth coming up over the bareness, the kitten finding the floor. "Uh — yeah," she said, and added the part that made it a thing a girlfriend was offering and not a thing she had wanted on sight. "Yeah. I like her."

But she had already told him — with the dropped stride, before the editing. He had read her right. He had read her right all afternoon, and the maple was burning, and the redheaded girl by the doors brought her chin back down out of the laugh and said something to her friend, and the two of them started off across the front of the dining hall toward the path that ran down to the science quad — unhurried, ambling, the friend talking, the redhead listening with her head tipped — with maybe a hundred feet of brick and lawn before they'd be gone.

"Go meet her," Pace said.

She looked at him then. Now, after — not before, not to be told what to want, but after the wanting had already shown, the social animal catching up and turning to him with the question that had nothing to do with whether she liked the girl and everything to do with whether she was allowed: *is this one mine to take.* He smiled at her. He nodded once, and tipped his chin toward the two girls already drawing away across the lawn. *Go.* He was happy, plainly and simply; the game was working, the gift was moving toward her hands, and the last thing she needed before she went was to see in his face that it was hers to take. He let her see it.

She went.

Pace did not follow. He found a light pole at an unhurried angle to the lawn and put his shoulder against it and watched, far enough back to be nobody, a man waiting on someone. He could see all of it and hear none of it, which suited him.

And it was a thing to watch. He had known she moved well — he had danced with her, felt the trained instrument of her under his hand — but he had not yet seen her aim it at a person. She did not hurry after the two girls and she did not angle at them in any way a watching eye could have named. She simply drifted, adjusting nothing he could point to, her line and the redhead's line bending toward each other by degrees so gradual they looked like chance, like two leaves on the same slow current, until the geometry that had been arranged across thirty feet of lawn arrived all at once and the shoulders met. A small collision. Nothing. And the beautiful part, the part he'd have stood there an hour to see again: it was the redhead's hand that flew to her own mouth. It was the redhead who pulled up short, stricken, apologizing — he could read the *oh-I'm-so-sorry* in the lift of her shoulders and the tilt of her head as clearly as if it had been printed. Two decades of dance and four years of whatever sorority actually taught under the mixers and the letters, all of it folded down into three seconds of execution so clean it looked like nothing — looked, if anything, like the redhead had walked into *her*.

Then Randi's hand went to the girl's arm — *no, no, my fault, it's fine* — light, warm, settling, the hand staying a half-beat longer than the apology required, and the redhead's shoulders came back down out of their fluster, and they were laughing. The friend laughed too. Names, now: he watched the little ritual of it, the heads, the small social weather of three people discovering they could stand to talk for a minute. Two minutes, in fact. He stood at his pole and watched his afternoon's work talk to its result and felt the particular contentment of a thing going exactly the way it should. Then the redhead and her friend said the thing people say, and lifted hands a little, and went on toward the science quad, and Randi turned and came back across the lawn to him.

She was failing to hide the grin. It was all over her, the grin, breaking the managed surface the way the dropped stride had, the way the bare *yes* had — her body out ahead of her composure again, lit up.

"Well," Pace said.

"Her name is Vee." She arrived in front of him glowing. "And we've got a stats class together."

Her feet did a small thing on the brick, a half-second of happy footwork she didn't decide to do, there and gone. He nodded, slow, and let her see the pride in it — *good, that's very good, look what you did.* The gift had a name now and a class schedule and a copper head he could still pick out, small, at the far edge of the lawn.

"Let's go feed you," he said.
