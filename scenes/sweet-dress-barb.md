# Sweet

The house was warm the way he kept it warm when he knew someone was coming, the heat turned up past comfort so no one would have to think about being cold. The sun porch held the last of the afternoon. By this hour the light came in low and went gold across the boards and lay along the dress on the form, and the burgundy took it and deepened, the plum coming up under the red the way it did, a wine-dark color with something moving in it. He had the side closure in his lap and was working it by hand, the small blind stitches that would not show, when he heard the bolt go home behind her.

She always locked it. He left the door off the latch for whoever was expected and she sealed it the moment she was inside, first thing, before her coat was off. He had stopped noticing that he noticed.

"You'll go blind," Randi said. She crossed the porch and stood at his shoulder and looked at the dress on the form the way she looked at most things, putting a price on it. Her eyes went over the silk and the seamless side and the hem. "It's sweet," she said. The warmth in it was real; the warmth was always real. "That he made it for her. By hand and all."

He set the last stitch and bit the thread. The word had a shape to it, a place it was built to go, the soft spot in a man that *sweet* was cut to find, and he felt for it the way you feel a sore tooth with your tongue, and there was nothing there to press. He liked the making. The silk had cost him a drive and the lining had cost him a night he had not minded losing, and that the cost was his hands and not his money was the whole of the pleasure; he could no more be shamed out of it than out of the truck. So the word went down into him and kept going and found no floor, and he watched her listen for the landing that did not come, the small blank half-second where the warmth she had brought had nowhere to sit.

"It'll fit her," he said, and laid the dress over the form, and that was true, and he stood.

She came down the hall after him. In the bedroom the lamp was on and the light was small and close, and she undressed without ceremony and put him on his back and got over him, her knees at his hips, her hands flat on his chest, with the close private concentration she brought to his body. Her hair was down. She had taken the band out somewhere between the porch and the bed and it hung loose around her face when she leaned into him, which it almost never did.

She moved on him slowly, watching his face. He kept his hands at her hips and let her set it. He could feel the thing rising in her before she said it, the way he had felt the dress-word go down, something coming up through her that she had decided to walk toward.

"How good is it," she said. "With Vee." Light, conversational, her hips not breaking their rhythm. "It's good. I know it is. Tell me how good."

He brought a hand up and laid it along her jaw, his thumb at the wet of her mouth, and held her eyes. "I'm here," he said. "With you. Now."

The sound she made was not a laugh and was close to one. It was not the thing she had asked for and she took it in like a touch anyway, her breath catching, her rhythm stumbling and finding itself. But she came back to it. She always came back to a thing once she had set out for it.

"That's not an answer." She bent down over him, the loose hair closing around them both, her voice dropping. "Is she better. Tell me she's better than me."

He read her the way he read everyone, in the body and in the present tense, the heat climbing in her as she pressed at the sore place herself, the flush going down her throat, her breath gone short and high. He understood what she was asking for and that the asking was already the having. And he understood, the way he had understood once before, out in the open with her stride breaking under her and a single word out of her before she could stop it, that the thing she could not say out loud could still be handed to her if he was the one to say it. He gathered the loose hair off her face and held it at the back of her skull and drew her down close.

"She's going to be perfect," he said, low, against her mouth. "Your first woman."

She went over all at once. It came up out of her and took her whole, her body closing hard around him, the cry torn loose before she could put anything over it, and he held her through it with his hand in her hair and felt the rightness of it settle in his chest, clean and final, the way the last stitch settles a seam. He had read her right. He had read her right from the start.

He did not think about it after. There was nothing in it to think about. She had told him yes with everything she had, and a yes that complete does not leave a question behind it. He smoothed the damp hair back off her face and she let him, her eyes closed, her breath going long. In the other room the light went down off the silk, and neither of them got up to put a lamp on, and for a while she stayed where she was, over him, in the last of the warmth he had made against her coming.
