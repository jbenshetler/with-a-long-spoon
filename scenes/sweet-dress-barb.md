# Sweet

The house was warm the way he kept it warm when he knew someone was coming, the heat turned up past comfort so no one would have to think about being cold. The sun porch held the last of the afternoon. By this hour the light came in low and went gold across the boards and lay along the dress on the form, and the burgundy took it and deepened, the plum coming up under the red the way it did, a wine-dark color with something moving in it. He had the side closure in his lap and was working it by hand, the small blind stitches that would not show, when he heard the bolt go home behind her.

She always locked it. He left the door off the latch for whoever was expected and she sealed it the moment she was inside, first thing, before her coat was off. He had stopped noticing that he noticed.

He laid the dress back over the form and went through the den to meet her. He took her face in both hands and kissed her, unhurried, and felt her settle into it before she stepped back. He had opened a bottle when she texted that she was leaving, and it had stood breathing on the side table the half hour since; he poured her a glass and put it in her hand, and there was the board he had set out beside it, the good cheese and a few black figs split open to the red. She let him tend her. She let herself be tended here, which she did nowhere else, and he liked the doing of it more than he would have said.

She took the wine and drifted with it, and her eye went to the dress the way it went to most things, putting a price on it. "It's sweet," she said. The warmth in it was real; the warmth was always real. "That you made it for her. By hand and all."

He felt for the place the word was built to find, the soft spot in a man that *sweet* was cut to reach, the way you feel a sore tooth with your tongue, and there was nothing there to press. He liked the making. The silk had cost him a drive and the lining a night he had not minded losing, and that the cost was his hands and not his money was the whole of the pleasure; he could no more be shamed out of it than out of the truck. So the word went down into him and kept going and found no floor, and he watched her listen for the landing that did not come, the small blank half-second where the warmth she had brought had nowhere to sit.

"It'll fit her," he said, easy, and set his hand at the small of her back, and that was as far as the word was going to get.

When she had had enough of the wine he took the glass from her hand and led her back along the hall to the bedroom, where the lamp was on and the light was small and close. He undressed her there himself and took his time at it, and her hair came loose somewhere in it and fell black and shining around her face the way it almost never did. Then she put him on his back and got over him, her knees bracketing his hips, her hands flat on his chest, with the close private concentration she brought to his body.

She moved on him slowly, watching his face. He kept his hands at her hips and let her set it. He could feel the thing rising in her before she said it, the way he had felt the dress-word go down, something coming up through her that she had decided to walk toward.

"How good is it," she said. "With Vee." Light, conversational, her hips not breaking their rhythm. "It's good. I know it is. Tell me how good."

He brought a hand up and laid it along her jaw, his thumb at the wet of her mouth, and held her eyes. "I'm here," he said. "With you. Now."

The sound she made was not a laugh and was close to one. She took it like a touch and gave it back. She bent low over him and dragged the loose black silk of her hair slow across his chest and his throat and his mouth, and kissed him down through it, so that he had the dark and the scent of it shut over his face. Then she lifted just clear of his mouth. But she came back to the question. She always came back to a thing once she had set out for it.

"That's not an answer." Her voice had dropped. "Is she better. Tell me she's better than me."

He read her the way he read everyone, in the body and in the present tense, the heat climbing in her as she pressed at the sore place herself, the flush going down her throat, her breath gone short and high. He understood what she was asking for, and that the asking was already the having. And he understood, the way he had understood once before, out in the open with her stride breaking under her and a single word gone out of her before she could stop it, that the thing she could not say aloud could still be handed to her if he was the one to say it. He gathered the hair off her face and held it at the back of her skull and drew her down close.

"She's going to be perfect," he said, low, against her mouth. "Your first woman."

She went over all at once. It came up out of her and took her whole, her body closing hard around him, the cry torn loose before she could put anything over it. He had meant to have the whole afternoon of her, to hold off and draw it long, to spend her and spend her again before he let himself near the edge of it. What took the plan out of his hands was the sound of her want arriving uncovered, the thing coming up out of her with nothing left to dress it in; it reached down into him and pulled him off his footing all at once. He set his jaw and held on, the long plan gone out of it, his hands locking at her hips and keeping her on him through the shaking until the last of it had gone through her. Only then did he let go and follow her over, sooner by far than he had meant, his grip going tight and then slack, his breath breaking against her hair.

He came back to the room slowly, with her gone heavy on his chest. He smoothed the damp hair off her face and she let him, her eyes closed, her breath going long, and he reached and drew the blanket up over the small of her back where the room would otherwise find her. In the other room the light went down off the silk, and neither of them got up to turn on a lamp, and for a while she stayed where she was, over him, in the last of the warmth he had made against her coming.
