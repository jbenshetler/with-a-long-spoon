The gravel ran out the last of itself under the tires and the truck came up into a clearing, a meadow gone tawny and flat with winter, and at the top of it the cabin stood waiting for them.

She hadn't known what to expect — something rented and charmless, maybe, a laminated card of rules tacked by the door — and it was not that. It was built board-on-board, the outer planks weathered to a soft silver-gray and gone darker, almost to pewter, along the seams where the damp sat longest; here and there a board stood a shade off from the ones around it, newer, set in at some point by a hand that had cared whether the place lasted. The wood had shrunk over its years, so that fine lines of shadow ran down between the boards, and a few of the edges had begun to check and splinter where the weather got at them. At the corners the battens were plain and square and true. A masonry chimney climbed one end of it. The windows were shuttered, blind. The whole of it had the particular dignity of a thing built well and plainly and then left to age into itself, and she thought, before she had quite decided to, that it was the sort of place he would choose — that it looked, somehow, like something he might have made.

She got down out of the truck and the cold came up to meet her, cleaner and harder than the cold in town, with an edge on it the valley took off the air down there.

"You said rustic," she said, looking up at it. "Somewhere past Christiansburg I decided rustic meant a lean-to. A tarp and good intentions. I'd made my peace with it."

"It's rustic," he said, mild, like he was standing by the word.

"It is not. It's —" she gestured at the whole of it and gave up on the sentence. "You don't get to call this rustic. There are people who'd kill for your lean-to."

And then the quiet got her, and she stopped. She stood a moment inside it. He carried his case and the grocery bags. She took her overnight bag, and then, because he asked her to, leaned back into the cab and drew out from under a folded blanket a picnic basket she hadn't known was riding behind them the whole way.

A door at the back stood open on a small bathroom, a shower. The furniture was old and local and good — hardwood gone dark and close-grained and satiny with handling, the joints still tight, a chest whose drawers she somehow knew would run true if she pulled them.
