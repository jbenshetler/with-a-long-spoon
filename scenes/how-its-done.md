# How It's Done

---

The Mercedes was at the curb when Vee came down, top already folded back, Randi behind the wheel in sunglasses with one wrist draped over the top of the door. The car was long and low and a green so dark it read as black until the sun moved on it, and it sat at the curb the way expensive things sit, without apology, taking up exactly the room it intended to.

Randi pushed the sunglasses up into her hair and smiled at her, and it was different. Vee felt it before she could have named it. The smile was gentler, the performance gone out of it, the edges soft. She had seen Randi's other smile, the bright even one that took a whole room in at a stroke, that Randi turned on her sisters and had turned once on Cassie clear across the coffee shop, generous and the same every time. This one was slower than that, and quieter, and it stayed a beat past when it might have stopped. Her eyes went down Vee and came back up, quick, taking her in like the first good thing of a day that had been built around it. "*Look* at you." Warm, approving, a little lit. "That belt — yes. Get in, get in — are you hungry? Tell me you're hungry."

Vee warmed under it and told herself not to make too much of it, as she always had to. Maybe she was inventing the difference because she wanted it that badly. But it did seem less performed, a thing spent rather than handed around — and there was the smoothie place still to set against the doubt, Randi in front of Brooke and all of them, choosing Vee at a cost, for a reason Vee had never once been able to locate. It had helped. It had not quite finished the job.

Vee got in, and the car closed around her. She had never been inside it before and was not ready for the inside of it. When she'd reached for the handle the window had dropped itself half an inch to meet the door, silent, on its own, and she'd startled at it; and when she pulled the door shut the glass rose back up and sealed itself into the frame with a soft precise sound, like the car was tucking itself in around her, and she thought, *of course it does that, of course a car like this does that.* The seat took her weight and gave it back cradled. The smell came up all around her, leather, warm and deep and animal, the smell of a hide that had been worth money and cared for since. The dash was a low dark curve of wood and dials. Everything she touched gave slightly and then held. She thought of the Corolla, the cloth seats she kept clean, her father saying *keep the mileage down, it didn't come for free,* and she set the thought where she set those thoughts.

Randi pulled out without checking whether Vee was ready, which was its own kind of statement, and they went down the long drive past the dorms with the top down at eleven in the morning. The wind came over the windshield and got into Vee's hair at once, lifting it, throwing copper across her face so she had to hold it back with a hand — her hair was having a torrid affair with the wind, the whole boisterous length of it carrying on without her. Randi's hair moved only at the very ends, as if it were flirting with the wind and had decided how far it would go. The rest of it stayed where it was meant to, pulled back and bound at the nape, dark and finished.

Vee watched her drive. It was a thing she did, in the car — studied Randi the way you study what you'd like to learn: the unbothered wrist on the wheel, the clean line of the jaw, the wind finding the collar of the silk and laying it open another half-inch against her collarbone, and Randi never once reaching up to fix it, a person to whom the wind was simply permitted that much and no more. And the band at her wrist — white gold, plain and heavy, no stone, nothing to it but the weight — that Vee had never once seen her without: there in the workout clothes, there now against the silk, the one fixed thing in a wardrobe that changed itself daily. Vee kept her own boisterous hair pinned down with one hand and kept looking a beat too long, and then made herself look at the road instead of the glamorous girl.

Two girls Vee half-knew were on the steps of Hahn as they passed. One of them looked at the car, and then at Vee in the car, and held the look a beat past nothing — and Vee felt herself lift inside it, felt herself become, for that beat, a person who rode in cars like this with people like Randi. She sat in the feeling and did not let it show on her face. Randi appeared not to register the girls at all, or if she did they cost her nothing, and she drove them off campus one-handed, the other arm along the door, and Vee held her hair and let herself be driven.

---

The place was off Route 9, a freestanding building of red brick that sat by itself in its own lot the way restaurants from a certain decade did, low and single-story with a deep pitched roof. Inside it was dim after the sun, and cool, and the noise of the room arrived softened — the walls were hung with green velvet, dark as a billiard table, and the velvet ate sound, so that the lunch crowd's talk came through as a low textured hum with the hard edges taken off. Overhead the rafters were exposed beams, dark wood, and from somewhere that was not quite anywhere Sinatra was working through one of those old standards, smooth and worn-in — the one where he gives in to a woman he knows he shouldn't, calls it witchcraft, that ancient pull he wouldn't switch off even if he could — the kind of voice that had been playing in rooms like this one for fifty years and would go on playing.

It smelled like bread. Bread and garlic and something roasting, fat and dark, off in the kitchen — a smell that arrived in the body before the mind, that said *here is plenty* before you had read a word of the menu.

The host knew Randi. Not by name, exactly, but knew her, and it moved them past the front and back along a row of booths divided by high partitions, each one its own half-room, until they reached one near the back that was nearly an alcove — three sides of it enclosed, the velvet close, the hum of the rest of the room held off at the partition's edge. You could have said anything in that booth. The room was built so that you could.

Randi let the cream cashmere slide off her shoulders as she sat, draping it over the back of the booth in a single motion, and underneath it the champagne silk caught what light there was, a thin pale shine at her chest where the camisole cut low. Vee looked, and then registered that she'd looked, and turned it into the thought she had a use for: that Randi had the body the magazines meant, small and high and the proper scale, the body you were graded against — the kind of body that got to wear a camisole cut to *there* and have it read as elegant instead of as a girl showing off what she couldn't help having. Vee's own chest, under the safe black, did not do elegant. She picked up her menu. Randi settled into the corner of the booth like the booth had been waiting for her, gold and pale silk and cream, lit somehow in the dim room the way money is lit.

Vee sat across from her and was aware of her shoes. They were her good black pumps, the only nice pair she owned, pointed and a little high, and they had been a touch tight on the brick walk from the lot and they were too much for a Sunday lunch and she had known it putting them on and had put them on anyway because there had been nothing else that felt like enough for a place she had not seen yet and had correctly guessed would be like this. There was a scuff at the toe of the left one she had tried to rub out that morning. She tucked her feet back under the booth.

The menus were heavy, cream card stock, and Vee opened hers and did the thing she did, which was to read the right side first. The column of prices. She read it the way you read a road in fog, looking for what was passable. The egg dishes were the low end and the low end was not low. She found a soup. She found that there was, in fact, just coffee, if it came to that.

"The salmon here is stupid," Randi said, not looking up. "Poached. They do it with this — whatever, it's good. I'll have that." She closed the menu. She had read, it seemed, only the left side, the way a person reads who has never once in her life had to find the bottom of a column. "And a glass of the Rombauer."

The waiter wrote it down without comment.

Vee looked at the wine list, which was its own card, and found the Rombauer on it, and found the number beside it, which was more than her soup and more than her soup and a coffee and more, she thought, than everything she was going to allow herself to order put together, for one glass, and she put the wine card down and did not pick it back up.

"I'll do the tomato soup," she said. "And water's great, thanks."

"You sure? Get a real drink, it's Sunday."

"If I have wine at noon I'll be facedown in the stacks by two and they'll have to check me out at the desk like overdue reserve." It came easy — the light quick thing that made people smile, that had always made people smile. "Water's perfect."

"Overdue reserve," Randi repeated, delighted, like Vee had handed her a present. "God, I love you. Okay." She leaned back into the cashmere and the corner and turned the full warm beam of her attention on, all at once, like a room light coming on. "So. Guess what I got up to Saturday."

"I'm not guessing, I'm going to get it wrong and you'll be insufferable."

"Guess anyway."

"Randi. I have only known you a few weeks. You could have robbed a bank — that I'd believe — but past that there is genuinely no telling. You're a woman of mystery."

Randi laughed, the real one, pleased. "Okay, fine, you're no fun and I'm dying to tell you anyway." She came in close over the table, conspiratorial, warm, bringing Vee in with her. "So I let this beautiful idiot take me home. Sweet, pretty, completely in love with himself — you know the kind, the kind you keep around for the view. And we get back to his place and I decide I want to play, so I have him tie my wrists to the headboard. With a *scarf.* Very romantic, very incompetent, the knot came loose twice." She was grinning, lit up with it, and the grin was an invitation. "And then he tickles me."

"He — he what?"

"Tickles me. Everywhere. And I can't move, that's the whole thing, my hands are tied up over my head and I am laughing so hard I genuinely thought I was going to *wet myself,* Vee, I'm not exaggerating, there were tears, I was gone — " the laugh breaking through her own telling now, pulling Vee bodily toward it " — and right when I think I'm going to actually die, he stops. And he switches. To somewhere else *entirely.*" A lift of one eyebrow, the gap left wide open, generous, delighted. "And I went off like a *rocket.* Boom. Tied to a man's headboard with a Hermès scarf. Best thing that's happened to me all month."

Vee was laughing too, and under the laughing a heat had come up that she did not look at directly. It was the picture that did it — not the man, the man was nobody, but Randi, Randi naked and helpless and laughing, her wrists tied over her head and her control gone, that finished surface finally cracked all the way open — and the heat the picture put low in Vee was a heat she had no shelf for and would have died before naming at this table. So she did what she did. "Okay, well, I'm never letting you near a scarf of mine," she said, fast, bright, heading it off, "or a headboard, or — you know what, you're banned from my whole bedroom." It worked; Randi laughed and Vee felt the heat get a half-second of cover. Then, because she actually wanted to know, in a smaller voice: "Weren't you scared, though? Not being able to move?"

Randi looked at her, warm, taking her in, and Vee felt the small heat of having asked it at all. The question had come out before she'd weighed it, from nearer than she liked. Then Randi's warmth came down over it and made it nothing, and Vee let it be nothing.

"That's the whole point, baby," Randi said. Gently. Like she was handing Vee something across the table. "The letting go. You spend your whole life holding the reins so tight your hands cramp. And then somebody takes them, and you *can't* hold them even if you wanted to, and it turns out that's the only time some of us ever put them down." A beat — and then the smile came back and closed the thing up, light again, easy. "A little helpless can be a very good thing. Done right."

The song had ended and another like it had started, the same era, the same slow ache. The booth held its quiet around them. The smell of the bread came again from the kitchen.

"Anyway," Randi said, and lifted the water at last and drank, and set it down, and the full beam came back on, aimed and warm. "Enough about me. You've got a face on. You've had a face on since you got in the car." She propped her chin on one hand. "Something happened. Tell me everything. Start at the beginning and don't you dare clean it up."

And Vee, who had spent a day building the version she would tell — the dress, the burgundy, the romance of being made something by hand, all of it arranged and pressed and ready, the parts a person could say out loud over lunch and none of the parts she couldn't — felt the careful version rise up smooth and rehearsed, and felt, underneath it, the other thing shift and strain to get out, and started talking before she had decided which one she was going to let win.

"Okay, so." She heard her own voice do the thing it did for Randi, the slightly-up thing, the trying-to-sound-like-this-happens-to-me thing. "There's this induction coming up — honor society, it's a whole formal thing, I have to actually look like a person. And there was this dress in a magazine I'd been mooning over for weeks that I was never in a hundred years going to afford. And he's making it. For me. By hand. Burgundy silk, the real thing, he went and *found* the silk." She could hear how good it sounded, how clean, a lovely thing happening to a girl over lunch. "It's — it's kind of the most romantic thing anyone's ever done for me."

Randi listened the way she listened, which was completely — chin on her hand, eyes on Vee, going where Vee's animation took them, to her mouth when she talked, to her hands when they moved, down the line of her and back. There was something keyed-up in the attention today, a brightness wound a notch tighter than the coffee places had ever drawn out of her, though Vee could not have said so and did not try. It was, Vee thought, the nicest part of Randi, this. The way she made you feel like the sort of girl who got asked things and had answers worth hearing. Nobody else listened like that.

"Mm," Randi said, warm, settling in. "Start at the beginning. He measured you himself?"
"He measured me himself," Vee said. "It's not like a store, there's no — he does all of it. He had the house warm for me when I got there, he'd thought of that, that I'd be cold, so he turned everything up before I came so I wouldn't be." The memory came up around her as she said it and she let it, because it was a good one and because Randi's whole face was turned to her like a lamp. "And he had me take my top off. For the fit. And, Randi — I wasn't even embarrassed. He just made it the most normal thing in the world, like of course, like it wasn't anything, and I stood there and he looked at me and I was just — fine. I was completely fine."

She was rolling in it now, telling it the way you tell the best thing, slowing down on the good part, *I was fine, he looked at me and I was fine,* turning it over so it caught the light.

Across the table Randi was very still. She was smiling, still smiling, but something had gone tight behind it, some muscle she was holding, and her thumb had found the base of her water glass and was working the edge of it, turning it a slow degree against the cloth and back. Vee, surfacing for a second out of her own story, caught the tightness and the hand and did not know what to make of them. Her first thought, fast and cold, was *she thinks I'm awful* — that Randi was holding back a laugh, that any second the worldly face would break and Vee would see what she really thought of a girl who took her shirt off for a man she'd known seventeen days. Vee's stomach dropped toward it. And then she looked again and reached past it, the way you reach for the kinder thing because you want it to be true: maybe it wasn't a laugh she was holding. Maybe it was the opposite. Maybe Randi was *moved,* a little, holding back a smile, happy for her, the soft look an older friend gets when something lovely happens to you. That had to be it. That was the look. Vee felt herself settle, warmed, certain now that she was sitting across from someone glad for her.

"Oh," Randi said, and the tightness was gone, folded away somewhere, the beam full up again. She leaned in. "Oh, you *love* being looked at."

"I do *not* — "

"You do. Listen to you. *He looked at me and I was fine.*" She said it back in Vee's own voice, fond, delighted, and Vee felt her face go hot in a way that was not entirely unhappy. "There's nothing wrong with it, baby, God, I wish more girls had it. Most of them spend the whole time trying to disappear." She tipped her head, warm, conspiratorial, the room narrowing to the two of them. "So. What were you wearing while he looked at you?"

And there it was — the door, swung open and waiting, and the thing Vee had planned to walk straight past. She had meant to keep this part folded up. She had meant to say *he fitted the top* and move on to the silk, the color, the safe parts. But the question was so soft, and the booth was so close, and she had wanted — she realized she had wanted, the whole drive over, maybe for days — to tell someone. To tell *her.* The wanting got there before the plan did.

"Just — " her voice came out smaller, and higher, and she heard it go and couldn't stop it, dropping down into something hushed, leaning across the table the way you lean to give up a secret " — you know. To fit it right he had to — he needed the real measurements, the actual ones, so it would sit the way it's supposed to, so — " the words piling up vague and quick around the shape of the thing she wasn't saying, the cut-out plain in the middle of them.

"Vee." Warm. Patient. A little amused. "Nobody measures over a bra."

Vee looked at her.

"They don't, sweetheart. You can't get a true line through an inch of padding. Anybody who's had something made knows that — it's not a *thing,* it's just how it's done." She said it gently, the way you'd tell someone the salad fork goes on the outside, no judgment in it, only the easy authority of a person who'd had a great many things made. And the clench in Vee's chest let go, because if it was just *how it was done,* if everyone knew, if it was ordinary — then there was nothing to be ashamed of, then she hadn't done anything, then she could say it.

"The bra too," Vee said. "Yeah."

"There she is." Randi sat back an inch, pleased, and let it land, and then came the next thing, easy, like it followed naturally, because it did. "So how did he manage that *fabulous* bust? God." She looked frankly at Vee's chest, the way a woman looks at another woman's good feature, with open longing. "The tit goddess was good to you." 

Vee laughed, helpless, complimented, the heat in her face turning to pleasure — because this was the thing about Randi, she could make you feel like the lucky one, like the thing you'd been ashamed of your whole life was, all along, something to be glad of.

"He must've had his work cut out," Randi went on, light, turning her glass. "All that to get around. You said he's a looker, this man." A pause, just slightly lowered, just for the two of them. "Were there any — *challenges?*"

The word sat there with its little hook in it. And Vee, who had been warm and laughing a second ago, felt the floor tilt, because she knew exactly what *challenges* meant, it meant the thing she would never say, it meant how her body had answered the warm air on her bared skin and his hands near her and the whole impossible fact of being looked at — and her face was already betraying her, she could feel it, and her voice came out flat and defensive and too fast.

"It was cold in the house."

A beat.

"Cold," Randi said. Not a question. Just the word, set down on the table between them, turned over once. "Hmm."

And Vee — caught, and knowing she was caught, and unable in the swim of it to remember quite *how* she'd been caught, only that the ground had shifted somewhere a few sentences back and Randi had felt it shift — started to laugh. It came up out of her before she could decide anything about it, the laugh of the absolutely cornered, and she put her face in her hands for a second and then dropped them and gave up.

"Okay," she said, through the giggle, "okay — actually it was really *hot.*"

And Randi laughed too, and the two laughs went up together over the table — Vee's first, raucous, her whole body in it, the laugh of someone setting down something she'd carried up too many stairs; and Randi's a half-beat behind and folding right around it, big, warm, the perfect sorority laugh.

"*Hot,*" Randi repeated, savoring it, wiping under her eye though there was nothing there. "Oh, you're *trouble.*" She let the laugh ebb, and stayed leaned in, and her voice came down again into the warm low register, the just-us register, the one Vee would have followed anywhere. "Okay. No. Go on, though — don't stop, you said he was measuring you for a *dress.*" A wicked little smile. "Dresses don't stop at your gorgeous chest."

"Randi."

"They *don't.* Keep going. He had to get down on his knees to do the rest of you, didn't he — with you up on the little platform. The pedestal. They put you up on a literal pedestal." She said it like it delighted her, like it was the best detail yet. "I bet that felt *great.* Yeah?"

"...Yeah," Vee said, and heard her own voice go soft and faraway, because it had felt great, it had felt like nothing she had words for.

"I bet he started at your hip. Didn't he."

"Yeah."

"Which hip?"

"The left one."

"Oh," Randi said, with enormous satisfaction, "all the best ones start at the left hip," which meant nothing at all and landed like a benediction, and Vee felt warmed and confirmed and led, one small *yeah* at a time, down and down. "And he's working his way around you, taking his time, getting it right. And then." Randi's voice dropped to almost nothing, the wink in it, the whole room gone. "Then he's right in front of you. On his knees. Looking up. And what did he *see?*"

Vee had gone dreamy. The booth was very far away. She was back on the platform with the warm air on her bare skin and his face turned up and the thing that had happened in her body that she had never told a single soul, not Cassie, not anyone, the thing she had folded up the smallest and pushed the deepest — and it rose now, all on its own, because the room was so safe and Randi was so close and the wanting to *give* it, finally, to set it down on the table with everything else and be laughed gently over and forgiven — the wanting opened her mouth.

"I — " Vee started. "There was — "

"You don't have to tell me."

It was gentle. It was kind. It was a door closing very softly.

And the laugh did not come. That was the thing Vee felt without being able to name — that here, where every other time the laugh had come up between them and joined them, there was instead a clean silence, and her secret was still half out of her with nowhere to go, risen and reaching and met with nothing.

Randi sat back. She let out a long breath, the air going out of the closeness all at once, and reached up and tucked a strand of the dark hair back where it lived, and when she spoke again her voice was a different voice — easy, ordinary, daylight. The hush was simply over.

"Big day," she said, and smiled, and looked around for the check.

---

The check came in a leather folder the color of the booth. Randi reached for it — "oh, let me" — but Vee already had her card out, as she always did, because the one thing she would not do at this table was let Randi buy her seat at it.

"You're sure?"

"I'm sure, it's nothing." It was not nothing. Vee laid the debit card on the folder and felt the little familiar drop as she let go of it, the awareness of the number it was speaking to, the number that would be smaller tonight in a way she would feel on Thursday. The glass of wine alone had cost more than everything Vee had eaten. She did not do the arithmetic out loud. She smiled and signed the slip and tipped well, because the other thing she would not do was be the broke girl who tipped badly, and she put the card away.

Randi watched her do all of it, warm and easy, and did not reach for the folder again.

---

The top was still down. They came back across town with the late sun coming sideways and the wind doing what it did to Vee's hair, and Randi reached over and turned the stereo up, and it was one of those songs — the ones that lived in every girl's body already, that had gotten all of them onto every floor at every party for years, the first four notes enough — and the energy came up off the road and the music and the speed and washed the whole heaviness clean away. Vee sang. Randi sang. For the length of the song Vee was not a girl who'd been turned inside out over tomato soup; she was a girl in a beautiful car in the sun with the most glamorous woman she knew, and the lunch behind her had been, she was already deciding, the best one of her life. The two of them at the light, singing, the wind everywhere — Vee caught a couple in the crosswalk looking, and did not mind being looked at, not one bit.

Randi pulled up in front of the dorm and stopped the car in the fire lane like fire lanes were a thing that happened to other people.

"This was perfect," Vee said. "Thank you. Really."

"Come here."

And Vee leaned across the gearshift to do the quick thing, the cheek thing, the goodbye — and it was not that. Randi's hand came up and found the hinge of her jaw, light, just resting there, just steadying, and kissed her on the mouth. Not the dry tight press Vee gave her grandmother. Soft, and in no hurry, and then — just as Vee thought it was ending — not ending: a small drawing-back, the barest pause, and then back again with a little more, a second press that had something in it the first one hadn't, before it let her go.

"See you soon, gorgeous."

Vee got out. The car pulled away down the fire lane, and the sound of it thinned into the ordinary evening, and Vee stood on the curb.

The smell of her had come away too, caught in Vee's hair and at her own jaw where the hand had rested, something bright and green she had no name for, there and then gone when she turned her head to find it.

She put two fingers to her mouth without deciding to.

*French,* she thought. *It's a French thing, she does the kiss-kiss, she's been everywhere, that's all it is, that's just how women like her say goodbye.* She had it explained away almost before her mouth was her own again.

And then a colder thought, all at once: had anyone *seen?* She turned and looked at the courtyard — empty, gone gold in the low sun, nobody on the benches. She looked up at the dorm, the bank of windows, all those lit and unlit squares with anyone behind any of them, and her heart went fast, scanning, counting the dark ones, the ones where someone could have been standing, looking down, at the car in the fire lane and the girl leaning across the seat and what the girl had leaned across the seat to do. She stood there a long moment, the cold thing spreading, before she could make herself stop looking, and go in.
