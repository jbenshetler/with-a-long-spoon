# His Shirt

*Vee POV, close third. ~Thu Dec 10, pre-dawn, her room. The second of the three displacements — after Under the Rug, before the Christmas fight. Vee alone.*

---

The heat came up through the building after midnight, as it always did, and their room stayed cold anyway, the window cracked its two inches where Cassie kept it, and below her Cassie slept and Vee, up top, did not.

She'd been not-sleeping a long time. She had his shirt on over the one she slept in — worn like that for the hall, for the walk to the bathroom, for the mornings Cassie was up first and might look and wouldn't have to say anything, a girl too far gone in a boy, the look enough on its own. Over her own shirt it was just a thing she wore. It hid what it was.

She reached down to the rail for her phone, and its light was too much in the dark, and she turned it low. She found his name near the top, where it stayed. She held her thumb over the little field where the words would go, and waited for the words.

There weren't any. There was nothing she could put in there that would come back as the thing she needed, nothing she could ask him that had an answer he could type. You couldn't send a message and get out of a person the shape of what you were to them; there was no text that made it come clear; the ones she thought of were all small, and would be answered small and warm, and would leave her exactly here. She looked at the empty field until it was only a lit shape in her hand, and put the phone face-down on the rail.

It wasn't enough, the shirt held off her skin over the other one. She sat up in the low space under the ceiling and pulled them both off together, hers and his, and dropped hers down the side, and put his back on with nothing under it, the cloth straight onto her, the way she wore it when no one could see. She lay back down.

For a while it worked. It always worked. It was him — the clean warm of him, and under that the other thing, the sawdust and the salt of the work, the smell he came in the door with — and she breathed it off her own shoulder and it was all of him. She could have gone through it and named every part. The things he made with his hands. The rooms he kept warm for her. The plate he set down once he'd built the hunger in her. All of it was in the cloth, every last thing he did, and she breathed him in and none of it told her what she was to him.

And it was warm because she was warm. That was the thing she couldn't stop feeling once she'd felt it — the heat in the shirt was her own, given back. He wasn't in it. He was across town in his own bed, gone down easy as he always went, easy as a man with nothing grinding in him, and she was here holding a smell in the dark. It had promised him close and there was no one close. She had everything he did and not the one thing he wouldn't say, and there was no name in her for it, not the big one and not the small one, only every breath of him now and the silence at the center where the word should be.

She couldn't lie in it. She took it off, up over her head, and the cold of the room came down on her bare skin all at once, and it was worse. It was so much worse, and she did not put it back on. She lay there with nothing on her in the cold that was worse than the shirt, and stayed in it. His shirt she left where it fell, beside her, not folded, not set back on the pillow, down in a heap where a dropped thing lands; and below her Cassie breathed on, slow and easy and asleep; and the window went grey, and she did not sleep.
