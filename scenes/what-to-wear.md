# What to Wear

The athletic dorm had the better dining hall and everyone knew it. Same meal-plan contract as everywhere else, but they plated it like they meant it, and the windows ran floor to ceiling down the west side, so even at six the whole room went gold and forgiving. Kayla's theory was that you walked in like you'd always eaten there and nobody ever asked you a thing. Six weeks running, nobody had.

They had the corner table that caught the last of the light. Meg was working through a plate of something with too much cheese, steady about it, telling them with her fork about her boyfriend's roommate and a spectacularly stupid thing he'd done. Meg was the calm one, dark-haired and level, a girl who ate like she had all night and let a sentence finish before she picked it up. Kayla let nothing finish. She was round-faced and blonde, a head of curls that moved when she did, and she was always moving, her words coming in a bright unbroken stream you climbed onto rather than followed. Vee sat between them, laughing with her whole body, loud and easy, the kind that made the next table look over and then want in.

Across the room Theo lifted a hand at her, tray balanced, and she lifted hers back.

"Theo waved at you," Kayla said, before Vee's hand was back down.

"Theo waves at everyone."

"Theo does not wave at everyone. Theo asked you to the fall thing."

"In September. And I said let's be friends, and now we're friends, and he waves." Vee stole a fry off Kayla's tray. "He took it like a gentleman. He's nice."

"He's nice." Kayla watched Theo find a seat, mournful about it. "That's the tragedy. They're all nice until you don't want them and then they're wonderful."

Meg, who followed the Pace situation in installments like her shows, pointed the fork at Vee now. "Okay, but the shirt. You have to actually tell the shirt. You keep getting to it and going soft and skipping the middle."

The shirt. Vee felt the smile arrive before she'd cleared it.

"It started raining on the way down," she said. "Not raining. Dumping. And I'm soaked, I'm freezing, I'm shaking so hard my teeth are going, and he reaches behind the seat and hands me his shirt. His dry one. The one he'd packed for himself." She could hear the story getting better in the telling and did not care. "And then he gets out. Out. Back into the cold rain. So I can change. Stands there with his back to the truck, soaking to the skin, so I can have the dry one."

"Stop," Kayla said.

"I kept it."

"You kept his shirt."

"He didn't ask for it back."

Meg had gone quiet in the good way, chin in her hand. "And then he kissed you."

"No."

They both looked at her.

"In the truck?" Kayla said. "After all that, he didn't—"

"I was about to. I turned and he was right there, and he's shivering. He's so cold he can't get a word out. I'm not going to kiss a man who's about to go hypothermic on me, so I had him drive us home instead." She stopped, because Kayla had both palms flat on the table. "And then he pulls up under the overhang at the dorm so I won't have to cross the rain again. Dead in the fire lane, engine running, and I'm about to. Again. And a blue-and-white slides in behind us, lights going."

"No."

"One bark of the siren. Just to move us along." Vee shook her head slowly. "So he moves us along. Pulls out, the cop on his bumper the whole way to the road, gone. That was the end of that."

"So you've never—"

"Once. One time, the whole time. Second date, a couple weeks ago—"

"A couple weeks." Meg looked up from her plate. "You know exactly how many days ago. Tell us."

Vee laughed, caught out. "Twelve." She hadn't even had to count, and that set the two of them laughing. "Twelve days. Walking home from dinner. I told him flat out I don't kiss a man I've known two days. Then I backed him into a tree and we made out. For a while."

"Once." Kayla sat back and stared at her. "All that, and it's been once. Vee, that's insane."

"It's making me lose my mind, is what it is." Vee said it into the table. "And I can't even explain why, that's the worst part. He's not pushing. It's the opposite. He asks me first. The small things. What I want to order, and he waits for the real answer, and then that's exactly what happens, the way I said it." She turned the glass a slow half-turn. "It sounds like nothing. I can hear it sounding like nothing. And I'm the one who can't think straight, climbing the walls over a man asking me what I want for dinner." She lifted her head. "He's different. I know how that sounds."

Meg smiled into her cheese, not unkindly. "Everybody's different at the start. I said it about Danny. Word for word, I think. He's different." She shrugged, a girl easy in her own thing. "Sometimes they even are."

Vee let it go by. It didn't catch on anything.

Her phone buzzed on the table, face-up, and her eyes were on it before she chose to look. His name. She read it, read it again, and the fry in her hand was just a thing in her hand now.

Both of them were watching her face.

"Oh, something happened," Kayla said, sitting up. "Look at her. What. What is it."

"He's asking me to his house."

A beat, and then the table came apart.

"His house." Meg set her fork down. From Meg, that was an event.

"When, is it a Saturday thing, is it a whole day, wait, you've never been there, you have never once been to his house—" Kayla had Vee by the arm and was going for the phone with her free hand. "Let me see it. What does it actually say."

"I've never been." Vee held the phone out of reach. "This'd be the first time."

"So what does it say?"

"It says, would you come to my house Saturday. Two o'clock." She turned the phone over and back, as if the rest might have loaded. "That's it. That's the whole message."

"That's not a message, that's a ransom note." Kayla was scandalized at a professional level. "What do you wear? What did he say to wear?"

"He didn't say."

"He didn't say what to wear. You've kissed the man one time and he's not even telling you what to put on to come to his house."

"His house," Meg said, dry and delighted. "For what."

"I don't know for what." Vee was laughing now, the flustered kind, naming it before they could get there first. "That's what I'm telling you. It doesn't say for what. It doesn't say anything. It's the least information a human being has ever sent me and I have read it a thousand times."

And that was the truth of it, under the noise. She'd had the dinners and the trail. She'd had the shirt, folded on her pillow at this exact hour of the night. She'd had every piece of him she'd been handed, and never once been through his door, and now there was a door, and Saturday behind it, and not one word about what it was for. She turned the glass again. Her heart was doing something quick and light and stupid, and she let it.

"Saturday," Kayla announced, to the table, to the gold room, to the whole athletic dorm. "We're doing hair Saturday. I'm coming to your room. This is happening."

"It's probably dinner," Vee said.

"Two in the afternoon is not dinner." Kayla stole her fry back. "It's not lunch either. There's no meal at two, Vee. He's not asking you over to eat." She let it sit. "He wants you for the afternoon. Bring a change of clothes."

Meg was already asking what she even owned that said his house without saying trying too hard, and the light came down the long windows and went orange and then thin, and none of them got up to go.
