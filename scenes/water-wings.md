# Water Wings

*Early fall. Vee's POV. The desirability anchor — her body in the world, before the machinery. Vee + Cassie at the university pool.*

---

The rec pool made you wear a real suit — no gym shorts, no sports bra, a lifeguard at the desk to check — and Cassie fought her way into hers like it had insulted her family. "Who designed this? Genuinely. What man?" The suit was navy and plain and older than their friendship, and she wore it because it had a job to do and not because she liked it. Cassie did not go to a pool to be admired; she'd sooner put her boxy frame in a t-shirt and cut-offs. Nobody was going to look at Cassie in it. Cassie knew that, and it didn't bother her one bit.

Vee stood on the wet tile in the old, nostalgic smell of chlorine and floor cleaner, already in her green-and-white one-piece — the one she'd worn at sixteen, for synchronized swimming, when there'd been less of her to fill it. There was more of her now. That was the trouble with Vee's suit: it fit, and it gave her nowhere to hide.

Cassie banged her locker shut and they headed out, Cassie looping the coil wristband with the locker key over her wrist.

Vee coiled the towel as she set it down on the bench — neat, unhurried, a little bit for show — and put her face on with it. She'd worn that face onto a hundred pool decks: chin level, shoulders open, the walk of a girl who had *meant* to arrive. The only way to the water ran the length of the bleachers, so she took it like an entrance.

It came about halfway down, lobbed easy: "You won't need floaties with *those!*" — the bleachers breaking up behind it, the laugh that's really the guys applauding each other. Vee gave it a long blink, half her mouth pursing, less than a grimace. Then, further along, lazy as a summer breeze: "Legs for *days.*" That one got the eyeroll, unhurried, aimed at the ceiling. She had them banked.

Cassie said it low, just for the two of them, still walking. "Tossers." A beat. "They will never know the love of a woman." And Vee laughed — more than the joke had earned, the game face breaking on it, fifty yards of held breath going out at once. Cassie could have pitched it up into the bleachers; she had the lungs, used them all rec season. She'd kept it for Vee.

Cassie waded to the middle of her ribs and stopped, planted, arms spread along the surface like a woman leaning on a fence. This was as far as Cassie went, and the water knew it. "Go on," she said, chin at the deep. "I'll be right here. Standing. Like a person." So Vee went on — the floor sloping away, tile giving to a colder smoothness, and then the dark stripe painted across the bottom where the shallow gave out. She crossed it, let her feet lose the floor, took the breath she'd taken ten thousand times, and went under.

The room switched off. The applause-laughter, the lifeguard's radio, the flat boom of the big space, Cassie — all of it gone in the swallowing green, and there was only the cold coming up from below and the light coming down through the surface in slow sheets and her own heartbeat, close and unhurried. She hung with nothing holding her and nothing to hold. Weightless the way you only are down here. Something in her chest that had stood at attention all afternoon quietly sat down.

Her hands found the old shape before she asked them to — the slow scull, palms feathering the little figure-eight that keeps you down and turned and yours — and she let them, and turned, and looked up at the bright moving ceiling of the surface from underneath. Nobody up there could see her. Nobody down here wanted anything. She had loved this before she had a body to be a problem, and the water was the one part of it that had never been taken. She stayed until her lungs asked, and a beat past that — in the green and the quiet and the old uncomplicated belonging — and grieved, a little, for something she didn't let herself name.

She came up. The surface broke and the room came back with it — light and noise and chlorine and the whole business of having a body in a place where people were — and she blinked it into focus, a little slow, still half in the green.

Cassie was watching her. Not hard, not asking; just clocking that her friend had gone off somewhere and come back with her face on wrong. She didn't ask. Cassie never asked — asking was for people who wanted the sad version to get bigger. What she did — from ten feet off, without moving a step closer — was sink her cupped hands to the wrists and drive them forward, fast: the trick that *shoots* it, a low sheet of water leaping the whole gap to break light over Vee's head.

It barely got her. That was the genius of it — outrage out of all proportion to the actual amount of water, which was the point. Vee blinked it off and then she was coming, crossing the ten feet of deep in a few strokes she didn't have to think about, into the shallow where she could get her feet and both arms under it and give it back for real. Cassie took the wave full in the chest and did not so much as change expression, which was somehow the funniest thing in the world, and Vee heard herself make a sound she hadn't made in a while — the shriek-laugh, the ugly one — and dug in and got her again. Whatever she'd carried up from the bottom didn't survive it. You can't grieve and fight your best friend for a mouthful of pool at the same time; the splashing won. It always did, back before any of it. The body that was such a problem stayed up on the deck with the coiled towel, where it could wait.
