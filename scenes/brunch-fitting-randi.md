# The Brunch — Vee Tells Randi About the Fitting

*(SATC track. Opening movement: the pickup, the room, the menu/wine choreography, and Randi's tickling-bound opener into the handoff. The extraction proper — bust, "Cold?", the warm-house cup-tell, the wet-spot probe — is the second movement, drafted separately. Vee's POV.)*

---

The Mercedes was at the curb when Vee came down, top already folded back, Randi behind the wheel in sunglasses with one wrist draped over the top of the door. The car was long and low and a green so dark it read as black until the sun moved on it, and it sat at the curb the way expensive things sit, without apology, taking up exactly the room it intended to.

Randi pushed the sunglasses up into her hair and gave Vee the full smile, the one that arrived all at once and made you the only thing in the frame. "Hello, *girl.*" She looked her up and down, warm, approving. "You look *great.* That belt. Get in, get in — are you hungry? Tell me you're hungry."

Vee got in, and the car closed around her. She had never been inside it before and was not ready for the inside of it — the door shut with a sound like a vault, soft and total, and the noise of the campus was simply gone, and the seat took her weight and gave it back to her cradled, and the smell came up all around her, leather, warm and deep and animal, the smell of a hide that had been worth money and cared for since. The dash was a low dark curve of wood and dials. Everything she touched gave slightly and then held. She thought of the Corolla, the cloth seats gone shiny at the edges, her father saying *keep the mileage down, it didn't come for free,* and she set the thought where she set those thoughts.

Randi pulled out without checking whether Vee was ready, which was its own kind of statement, and they went down the long drive past the dorms with the top down at eleven in the morning. The wind came over the windshield and got into Vee's hair at once, lifting it, throwing copper across her face so she had to hold it back with a hand — her hair was having a torrid affair with the wind, the whole shameless length of it. Randi's hair moved only at the very ends, as if it were flirting with the wind and had decided how far it would go. The rest of it stayed where it was meant to, pulled back and bound at the nape, dark and finished.

Two girls Vee half-knew were on the steps of Hahn as they passed. One of them looked at the car, and then at Vee in the car, and held the look a beat past nothing — and Vee felt herself lift inside it, felt herself become, for that beat, a person who rode in cars like this with people like Randi. She sat in the feeling and did not let it show on her face. Randi appeared not to register the girls at all, or registered them the way you register weather, and drove them off campus one-handed, the other arm along the door, and Vee held her hair and let herself be driven.

---

The place was off Route 9, a freestanding building of red brick that sat by itself in its own lot the way restaurants from a certain decade did, low and single-story with a deep pitched roof. Inside it was dim after the sun, and cool, and the noise of the room arrived softened — the walls were hung with green velvet, dark as a billiard table, and the velvet ate sound, so that the lunch crowd's talk came through as a low textured hum with the hard edges taken off. Overhead the rafters were exposed beams, dark wood, and from somewhere that was not quite anywhere Sinatra was working through one of those old standards, smooth and unhurried — the one where he gives in to a woman he knows he shouldn't, calls it witchcraft, that ancient pull he wouldn't switch off even if he could — the kind of voice that had been playing in rooms like this one for fifty years and would go on playing.

It smelled like bread. Bread and garlic and something roasting, fat and dark, off in the kitchen — a smell that arrived in the body before the mind, that said *here is plenty* before you had read a word of the menu.

The host knew Randi. Not by name, exactly, but in the way that mattered, the recognition that moved them past the front and back along a row of booths divided by high partitions, each one its own half-room, until they reached one near the back that was nearly an alcove — three sides of it enclosed, the velvet close, the hum of the rest of the room held off at the partition's edge. You could have said anything in that booth. The room was built so that you could.

Randi let the cream cashmere slide off her shoulders as she sat, draping it over the back of the booth in a single motion, and underneath it the champagne silk caught what light there was, a thin pale shine at her chest where the camisole cut low, and she settled into the corner of the booth like the booth had been waiting for her. The gold of her trousers, the pale silk, the cream cashmere behind her — she was lit, somehow, in the dim room, the way money is lit.

Vee sat across from her and was aware of her shoes. They were her good black pumps, the only nice pair she owned, pointed and a little high, and they had been a touch tight on the brick walk from the lot and they were too much for a Saturday lunch and she had known it putting them on and had put them on anyway because there had been nothing else that felt like enough for a place she had not seen yet and had correctly guessed would be like this. There was a scuff at the toe of the left one she had tried to rub out that morning. She tucked her feet back under the booth.

The menus were heavy, cream card stock, and Vee opened hers and did the thing she did, which was to read the right side first. The column of prices. She read it the way you read a road in fog, looking for what was passable. The egg dishes were the low end and the low end was not low. She found a soup. She found that there was, in fact, just coffee, if it came to that.

"The salmon here is stupid," Randi said, not looking up. "Poached. They do it with this — whatever, it's good. I'll have that." She closed the menu. She had read, it seemed, only the left side, the way a person reads who has never once in her life had to find the bottom of a column. "And a glass of the Rombauer."

The waiter wrote it down without comment.

Vee looked at the wine list, which was its own card, and found the Rombauer on it, and found the number beside it, which was more than her soup and more than her soup and a coffee and more, she thought, than everything she was going to allow herself to order put together, for one glass, and she put the wine card down and did not pick it back up.

"I'll do the — the tomato soup," she said. "And water's fine."

"You sure? Get a real drink, it's Saturday."

"Water's good. I have reading later," Vee said, which was true and was also the kind of thing you said.

"God, *reading,*" Randi said, with affection, as if reading were a charming hardship that happened to other people, and she leaned back into the cashmere and the corner and looked at Vee with the full warm beam of her attention, which was a thing that happened all at once when it happened, like a room light coming on. "Okay. So. Guess what I got up to Saturday."

"What."

"No, guess."

"Randi."

"Fine, I'll just tell you, you're hopeless." She was already smiling, already into it, turning her water glass a slow quarter-turn on the cloth with two fingers without lifting it. "So I let this guy take me home. Cute, very into himself, fine — you know the type, the type that's a project. And we get back to his place and I tell him I want to try something, and I have him tie my wrists. To the headboard. Nothing fancy, it was a *scarf,* it was very amateur hour." She tipped her head, the dry amusement coming up under the words. "And then he tickles me."

"He — tickles you."

"Tickles me. Everywhere. And I cannot move, that's the whole thing, my hands are up and tied and I'm laughing so hard I genuinely thought I was going to wet myself, I'm not even kidding, I was *gone,* there were tears — " She was laughing now, the real version, and it pulled Vee toward it. "And right when I'm about to actually die, he stops. And he switches. To — somewhere else entirely." A lift of one eyebrow, the gap left open, deliberate, elegant. "And I went off like a *rocket.* Boom. Tied to the headboard. I have never. In my life."

Vee was laughing too, the warmth of it, and a heat she did not look at directly coming up underneath the laughing. She turned her own water glass without meaning to, the way Randi was turning hers.

"Weren't you — " Vee started, and didn't know exactly what she was asking, and asked the nearest true thing. "Didn't it freak you out? Not being able to move?"

Randi looked at her. The look was kind and it was also doing something, reaching in past the question to whatever was under it, and Vee felt the look the way she had felt the girl's look on the Hahn steps, the sense of being seen sitting in a place she had not quite meant to sit.

"That's the whole point, baby," Randi said. Gently. Like she was handing Vee something across the table. "The letting go. You spend your whole life holding the reins so tight your hands cramp. And then somebody takes them, and you *can't* hold them even if you wanted to, and it turns out — " she turned the glass, set two fingers flat against its base " — that's the only time some of us ever put them down." She smiled, and the smile closed the thing back up, light again, easy. "A little helpless can be a very good thing. Done right."

The song had ended and another like it had started, the same era, the same unhurried ache. The booth held its quiet around them. The smell of the bread came again from the kitchen.

"Anyway," Randi said, and lifted the water at last and drank, and set it down, and the full beam came back on, aimed and warm. "Enough about me. You've got a face on. You've had a face on since you got in the car." She propped her chin on one hand. "Something happened. Tell me everything. Start at the beginning and don't you dare clean it up."

And Vee, who had spent two days deciding exactly how she would tell this — the dress, the burgundy, the romance of being made something, the parts a person could say out loud over lunch — felt the careful version arrange itself in her mouth, and felt, underneath it, the other thing shift and want out, and opened her mouth not entirely sure which one was going to come.

"So," Vee said. "He's making me a dress."
