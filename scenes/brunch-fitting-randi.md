# The Brunch — Vee Tells Randi About the Fitting

*(SATC track. Opening movement: the pickup, the room, the menu/wine choreography, and Randi's tickling-bound opener into the handoff. The extraction proper — bust, "Cold?", the warm-house cup-tell, the wet-spot probe — is the second movement, drafted separately. Vee's POV.)*

---

The Mercedes was at the curb when Vee came down, top already folded back, Randi behind the wheel in sunglasses with one wrist draped over the top of the door. The car was long and low and a green so dark it read as black until the sun moved on it, and it sat at the curb the way expensive things sit, without apology, taking up exactly the room it intended to.

Randi pushed the sunglasses up into her hair and gave Vee the full smile, the one that arrived all at once and made you the only thing in the frame. Her eyes went down Vee and came back up, quick, bright, taking her in like the first good thing of a day that had been built around it. "Hello, *girl.*" Warm, approving, a little lit. "You look *great.* That belt. Get in, get in — are you hungry? Tell me you're hungry." Vee glowed under it the way she always did; Randi looked at everyone like that, looked at the whole world like it was lucky to be in front of her, and being inside the beam for a minute was its own small warmth.

Vee got in, and the car closed around her. She had never been inside it before and was not ready for the inside of it. When she'd reached for the handle the window had dropped itself half an inch to meet the door, silent, on its own, and she'd startled at it; and when she pulled the door shut the glass rose back up and sealed itself into the frame with a soft precise sound, like the car was tucking itself in around her, and she thought, *of course it does that, of course a car like this does that.* The seat took her weight and gave it back cradled. The smell came up all around her, leather, warm and deep and animal, the smell of a hide that had been worth money and cared for since. The dash was a low dark curve of wood and dials. Everything she touched gave slightly and then held. She thought of the Corolla, the cloth seats gone shiny at the edges, her father saying *keep the mileage down, it didn't come for free,* and she set the thought where she set those thoughts.

Randi pulled out without checking whether Vee was ready, which was its own kind of statement, and they went down the long drive past the dorms with the top down at eleven in the morning. The wind came over the windshield and got into Vee's hair at once, lifting it, throwing copper across her face so she had to hold it back with a hand — her hair was having a torrid affair with the wind, the whole boisterous length of it carrying on without her. Randi's hair moved only at the very ends, as if it were flirting with the wind and had decided how far it would go. The rest of it stayed where it was meant to, pulled back and bound at the nape, dark and finished.

Vee watched her drive. It was a thing she did, in the car — studied Randi the way you study what you'd like to learn: the unbothered wrist on the wheel, the clean line of the jaw, the way the wind found the collar of the silk and laid it open another half-inch against her collarbone and Randi never once reached up to fix it, was a person to whom the wind was simply permitted that much and no more. Vee kept her own boisterous hair pinned down with one hand and kept looking at the open collar a beat too long, and then made herself look at the road instead of the glamorous girl.

Two girls Vee half-knew were on the steps of Hahn as they passed. One of them looked at the car, and then at Vee in the car, and held the look a beat past nothing — and Vee felt herself lift inside it, felt herself become, for that beat, a person who rode in cars like this with people like Randi. She sat in the feeling and did not let it show on her face. Randi appeared not to register the girls at all, or registered them the way you register weather, and drove them off campus one-handed, the other arm along the door, and Vee held her hair and let herself be driven.

---

The place was off Route 9, a freestanding building of red brick that sat by itself in its own lot the way restaurants from a certain decade did, low and single-story with a deep pitched roof. Inside it was dim after the sun, and cool, and the noise of the room arrived softened — the walls were hung with green velvet, dark as a billiard table, and the velvet ate sound, so that the lunch crowd's talk came through as a low textured hum with the hard edges taken off. Overhead the rafters were exposed beams, dark wood, and from somewhere that was not quite anywhere Sinatra was working through one of those old standards, smooth and unhurried — the one where he gives in to a woman he knows he shouldn't, calls it witchcraft, that ancient pull he wouldn't switch off even if he could — the kind of voice that had been playing in rooms like this one for fifty years and would go on playing.

It smelled like bread. Bread and garlic and something roasting, fat and dark, off in the kitchen — a smell that arrived in the body before the mind, that said *here is plenty* before you had read a word of the menu.

The host knew Randi. Not by name, exactly, but in the way that mattered, the recognition that moved them past the front and back along a row of booths divided by high partitions, each one its own half-room, until they reached one near the back that was nearly an alcove — three sides of it enclosed, the velvet close, the hum of the rest of the room held off at the partition's edge. You could have said anything in that booth. The room was built so that you could.

Randi let the cream cashmere slide off her shoulders as she sat, draping it over the back of the booth in a single motion, and underneath it the champagne silk caught what light there was, a thin pale shine at her chest where the camisole cut low. Vee looked, and then registered that she'd looked, and turned it into the thought she had a use for: that Randi had the body the magazines meant, small and high and the proper scale, the body you were graded against — the kind of body that got to wear a camisole cut to *there* and have it read as elegant instead of as a girl showing off what she couldn't help having. Vee's own chest, under the safe black, did not do elegant. She picked up her menu. Randi settled into the corner of the booth like the booth had been waiting for her, gold and pale silk and cream, lit somehow in the dim room the way money is lit.

Vee sat across from her and was aware of her shoes. They were her good black pumps, the only nice pair she owned, pointed and a little high, and they had been a touch tight on the brick walk from the lot and they were too much for a Saturday lunch and she had known it putting them on and had put them on anyway because there had been nothing else that felt like enough for a place she had not seen yet and had correctly guessed would be like this. There was a scuff at the toe of the left one she had tried to rub out that morning. She tucked her feet back under the booth.

The menus were heavy, cream card stock, and Vee opened hers and did the thing she did, which was to read the right side first. The column of prices. She read it the way you read a road in fog, looking for what was passable. The egg dishes were the low end and the low end was not low. She found a soup. She found that there was, in fact, just coffee, if it came to that.

"The salmon here is stupid," Randi said, not looking up. "Poached. They do it with this — whatever, it's good. I'll have that." She closed the menu. She had read, it seemed, only the left side, the way a person reads who has never once in her life had to find the bottom of a column. "And a glass of the Rombauer."

The waiter wrote it down without comment.

Vee looked at the wine list, which was its own card, and found the Rombauer on it, and found the number beside it, which was more than her soup and more than her soup and a coffee and more, she thought, than everything she was going to allow herself to order put together, for one glass, and she put the wine card down and did not pick it back up.

"I'll do the tomato soup," she said. "And water's great, thanks."

"You sure? Get a real drink, it's Saturday."

"If I have wine at noon I'll be facedown in the stacks by two and they'll have to check me out at the desk like overdue reserve." It came easy, the way it always came — the light quick thing that made people smile, that had always made people smile. "Water's perfect."

"Overdue reserve," Randi repeated, delighted, like Vee had handed her a present. "God, I love you. Okay." She leaned back into the cashmere and the corner and turned the full warm beam of her attention on, all at once, like a room light coming on. "So. Guess what I got up to Saturday."

"I'm not guessing, I'm going to get it wrong and you'll be insufferable."

"Guess anyway."

"Randi. I have known you a week. You could have robbed a bank — that I'd believe — but past that there is genuinely no telling. You're a woman of mystery."

Randi laughed, the real one, pleased. "Okay, fine, you're no fun and I'm dying to tell you anyway." She came in close over the table, conspiratorial, warm, bringing Vee in with her. "So I let this beautiful idiot take me home. Sweet, gorgeous, completely in love with himself — you know the kind, the kind you keep around for the view. And we get back to his place and I decide I want to play, so I have him tie my wrists to the headboard. With a *scarf.* Very romantic, very incompetent, the knot came loose twice." She was grinning, lit up with it, and the grin was an invitation. "And then he tickles me."

"He — he what?"

"Tickles me. Everywhere. And I can't move, that's the whole thing, my hands are tied up over my head and I am laughing so hard I genuinely thought I was going to *wet myself,* Vee, I'm not exaggerating, there were tears, I was gone — " the laugh breaking through her own telling now, pulling Vee bodily toward it " — and right when I think I'm going to actually die, he stops. And he switches. To somewhere else *entirely.*" A lift of one eyebrow, the gap left wide open, generous, delighted. "And I went off like a *rocket.* Boom. Tied to a man's headboard with a Hermès scarf. Best thing that's happened to me all month."

Vee was laughing too, and under the laughing a heat had come up that she did not look at directly. It was the picture that did it — not the man, the man was nobody, but Randi, Randi naked and helpless and laughing, her wrists tied over her head and her control gone, that finished surface finally cracked all the way open — and the heat the picture put low in Vee was a heat she had no shelf for and would have died before naming at this table. So she did what she did. "Okay, well, I'm never letting you near a scarf of mine," she said, fast, bright, getting out ahead of it, "or a headboard, or — you know what, you're banned from my whole bedroom." It worked the way it always worked; Randi laughed and Vee felt the heat get a half-second of cover. Then, because she actually wanted to know, in a smaller voice: "Weren't you scared, though? Not being able to move?"

Randi looked at her, warm, taking her in, and Vee felt herself land somewhere good — chosen, clever, the sort of girl who got to sit across from Randi and be asked things and have answers worth hearing.

"That's the whole point, baby," Randi said. Gently. Like she was handing Vee something across the table. "The letting go. You spend your whole life holding the reins so tight your hands cramp. And then somebody takes them, and you *can't* hold them even if you wanted to, and it turns out that's the only time some of us ever put them down." A beat — and then the smile came back and closed the thing up, light again, easy. "A little helpless can be a very good thing. Done right."

The song had ended and another like it had started, the same era, the same unhurried ache. The booth held its quiet around them. The smell of the bread came again from the kitchen.

"Anyway," Randi said, and lifted the water at last and drank, and set it down, and the full beam came back on, aimed and warm. "Enough about me. You've got a face on. You've had a face on since you got in the car." She propped her chin on one hand. "Something happened. Tell me everything. Start at the beginning and don't you dare clean it up."

And Vee, who had spent two days building the version she would tell — the dress, the burgundy, the romance of being made something by hand, all of it arranged and pressed and ready, the parts a person could say out loud over lunch and none of the parts she couldn't — felt the careful version rise up smooth and rehearsed, and felt, underneath it, the other thing shift and strain to get out, and started talking before she had decided which one she was going to let win.

"Okay, so." She heard her own voice do the thing it did for Randi, the slightly-up thing, the trying-to-sound-like-this-happens-to-me thing. "There's this induction coming up — honor society, it's a whole formal thing, I have to actually look like a person. And there was this dress in a magazine I'd been mooning over for weeks that I was never in a hundred years going to afford. And he's making it. For me. By hand. Burgundy silk, the real thing, he went and *found* the silk." She could hear how good it sounded, how clean, a lovely thing happening to a girl over lunch. "It's — it's kind of the most romantic thing anyone's ever done for me."

Randi listened the way she listened, which was completely — chin on her hand, eyes on Vee, going where Vee's animation took them, to her mouth when she talked, to her hands when they moved, down the line of her and back. There was something keyed-up in the attention today, a brightness wound a notch tighter than the coffee places had ever drawn out of her, though Vee could not have said so and did not try. It was, Vee thought, the nicest part of Randi, this. The way she made you feel like the most interesting person who had ever sat across from her. Nobody else listened like that.

"Mm," Randi said, warm, settling in. "Start at the beginning. He measured you himself?"
