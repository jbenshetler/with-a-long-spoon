The gravel ran out the last of itself under the tires and the truck came up into a clearing, a meadow gone tawny and flat with winter, and at the top of it the cabin stood waiting for them.

She hadn't known what to expect — something rented and charmless, maybe, a laminated card of rules tacked by the door — and it was not that. It was built board-on-board, the outer planks weathered to a soft silver-gray and gone darker, almost to pewter, along the seams where the damp sat longest; here and there a board stood a shade off from the ones around it, newer, set in at some point by a hand that had cared whether the place lasted. The wood had shrunk over its years, so that fine lines of shadow ran down between the boards, and a few of the edges had begun to check and splinter where the weather got at them. At the corners the battens were plain and square and true. A masonry chimney climbed one end of it. The windows were shuttered, blind. The whole of it had the particular dignity of a thing built well and plainly and then left to age into itself, and she thought, before she had quite decided to, that it was the sort of place he would choose — that it looked, somehow, like something he might have made.

She got down out of the truck and the cold came up to meet her, cleaner and harder than the cold in town, with an edge on it the valley took off the air down there.

"You said rustic," she said, looking up at it. "Somewhere past Christiansburg I decided rustic meant a lean-to. A tarp and good intentions. I'd made my peace with it."

"It's rustic," he said, mild, like he was standing by the word.

"It is not. It's —" she gestured at the whole of it and gave up on the sentence. "You don't get to call this rustic. There are people who'd kill for your lean-to."

And then the quiet got her, and she stopped. She stood a moment inside it. No road, no one — only the small tick of the cooling engine and the wind working in the dry grass and, past that, nothing at all. "I don't think I've ever heard it be this quiet," she said, lower now. "There's just — nothing."

"There's a lot," he said. "None of it's people."

He went up to the door with the key and it did not want to give. The frame had settled, or the door had swollen into it across some wet season, and he worked it without hurry, a shoulder to it and a lift on the handle and then a long steady push, the way he did everything, until it came free all at once and swung in.

He carried his case and the grocery bags. She took her overnight bag, and then, because he asked her to, leaned back into the cab and drew out from under a folded blanket a picnic basket she hadn't known was riding behind them the whole way.

Inside, it was nearly one room. A chair, a chest of drawers, a bed, a fireplace laid and ready, and along the far wall a kitchenette behind a counter that was the only thing setting it apart from the rest. A door at the back stood open on a small bathroom, a shower. The furniture was old and local and good — hardwood gone dark and close-grained and satiny with handling, the joints still tight, a chest whose drawers she somehow knew would run true if she pulled them. Wear on everything and nothing failing anywhere. It was warmer in than out, though not by much, and the air had the held, shut-up stillness of a place that had been standing empty, waiting to be come back to.

He left it going. It would have the cold beaten back by the time they wanted it.

They were hungry, and it had been a long way, and the last of the day was going fast. He took up the basket again and she brought the two heavy quilts down off the foot of the bed, and they went back out, onto the porch this time, and sat down side by side on the top step under the overhang, wrapped to the chins, looking out over the meadow with the cold all around them and the fire warming the wall at their backs.

"Did you make it?"

"Yes."

"That should not work," she said. "Cider and — that. Those should not be friends." "I wanted to please you," he said.

She let it go. She was good, now, at letting it go. She leaned her shoulder into his instead, warm under the quilt, and let that be the answer. It nearly was.

They finished and sat on with the cider going cool in their hands, and watched the evening come. The sun went down red behind the far ridge and took the last of the warmth with it, and the wind came up and worked in the dry grass and sent a few brown leaves skating out across the meadow, end over end, and there was no other sound anywhere — no road, no voice, nothing but the wind and the two of them and the small tick and settle of the cabin behind them. She had never been anywhere so quiet in her life. "You're cold," he said. "Come in."

They carried in the basket and the bowls and the cups, and the room had changed while they were gone. The fire had eaten the cold out of it; the windows had gone dark and the firelight had come up in their place, low and orange and moving on the walls, and the small space held the warmth the way the porch had held the cold. She stood in the middle of it with her hands aching and the heat coming at her all at once, almost too much, enough to sting her eyes.

He set the things down on the counter. Then he came to her and worked the top buttons of his flannel shirt open, three or four of them, taking his time, and reached for her hands.

"No — Pace, don't, they're freezing." She tried to pull them back, curling them in against herself. "You don't want these anywhere near you, I'm serious, I'm like a popsicle —"

"I know what they are." He took them anyway, both of them, easily, the way he took everything, and drew them in under the shirt and laid them flat against his bare chest. The shock of it went through both of them — her ice on his warmth — and she made a sound, half apology, and tried once more to take them back, and he held them there. He did not flinch from the cold of her the way another man would have. He kept her hands against the slow steady heat of him until the ache in her fingers began to loosen and let go, and she stopped pulling, and stood there with her palms on him and her eyes coming up to his.

"There," he said. "Not a popsicle."

She left her hands where they were, on the warm breadth of his chest, and tipped her face up, and it was the easiest thing in the world to kiss him. He kissed her back, unhurried, no part of him anywhere but here, and the warmth went on building between them, hand to chest and mouth to mouth, until it was its own thing in the room with them.

There was nowhere to be and nothing to rush toward. He undressed her slowly in the firelight and she let him, and he took his time over her the way he took his time over everything that mattered to him, and the cold weekend and the long winter and the whole hard tangle of December went off somewhere far away and left them alone. It was warm and unhurried and sure. Afterward they lay folded together under the quilts with the fire burning low across the room, and she listened to it tick and shift and settle, and to him breathing slow against her hair, and thought she could not remember ever being so warm, or so far from everywhere, or so entirely where she wanted to be.
