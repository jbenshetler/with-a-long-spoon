# The Induction

*Vee POV. November.*

Cassie walked her as far as the doors of the hall and no farther, because past them the honorees peeled off into their own roped row down front and everyone else went to the seats. "Go make your mother cry again," Cassie said, and took Vee's phone out of her hand to hold, and looked her up and down and said, "You look—" and didn't finish it, which from Cassie was a standing ovation. Then she went to find a chair, and Vee went down to the row and found her name on a card taped to the arm of a folding seat, and sat, and waited.

The room was high and full of the good afternoon light she'd let herself picture — long windows down one side, the light in bars along the floor, the podium, a woman from the department reading names into a microphone one at a time. She sat near the front in the burgundy, among girls in the safe department-store navy she'd pictured too. The black dress from the back of her closet, the one that had always been good enough, was still on its hanger at home; she hadn't needed it. Under the burgundy, nothing at all.

There couldn't be. She'd found it out in his mirror — the slit came open to the hip and the edge of anything showed, cut across the long line the dress made of her — so to wear the dress as it was built to be worn was to wear nothing under it, and she had wanted to wear the dress. So she'd come bare, and careful. She'd been careful the whole way here: the short managed steps that kept the slit lying shut, and now a hand kept low at her hip while she sat, holding the seam closed over her thigh, the small choreography of a woman with a secret keeping it. Getting ready it had felt like a private joke she was in on. It felt different now, three rows from a microphone, in a room full of somebody's parents.

The heels didn't help — four inches, a fine dark strap and not much else, and dear. Randi had found them, knelt and done the little ankle strap with two fingers and sat back to look, and Vee had stood there resettled forward onto the balls of her feet, because the dress wanted the height and she had stopped being able to tell the difference between what she wanted and what the dress wanted for her. Her toenails showed at the open ends of the heels, and her fingernails where they lay in her lap: a frosted plum that woke and threw the light when it moved, the same highlight the silk gave up when it shimmered.

Pace was not there. He'd been sorry about it in the quiet way he was sorry about things, no performance in it, the night the dates came clear — a mathematics conference in Berkeley, three days of it, the same three as the induction, and neither would move — he had made the dress for exactly this, her name read out in a good room, and he was going to miss the one wearing it was built for. She hadn't told him it was only a dress. It was the most anyone had ever made for her and they both knew it. She'd tried only a ceremony, and it had come out thin, because he'd been up the mountain with her the day she talked it up, cherries in her hand and the whole thing still ahead of her, and he remembered that, and so did she.

She'd built a picture of this — up the mountain the day she named the dress, and again in his bed with it going dark on the door — walking in late, on purpose, the room turning to look. But the actual room was warm and dull and kind, full of nobody's late entrance; the navy girls rose and crossed and came back with their hands full and no one watched them any longer than the walking took. No one was going to turn on their own. She sat with her hand at her hip and the slit shut and waited for her name.

Then they read it.

She stood, and the hem fell where it should because of the heels, and she went out toward the stage. Her first step was small — the careful one, the slit lying shut, safe. She could have taken the whole walk like that, mincing it modest all the way to the podium, and no one would have known there was anything to know.

She took a breath, and made the second step long.

The slit fell open off her bare thigh and up to the hip, and she gave the room her leg on purpose — the whole length of it, calf to hip, nothing under the silk and nothing to guess at, and she let it register down the rows, the small still beat where a warm dull room worked out exactly what it was looking at. She had good legs. She stalked across the stage giving them away a step at a time, the give and take of her in the good light, and did not once make herself small — and a murmur went through the crowd as she came, the sound a dull room makes when it wakes up.

And the heat came up — her body agreeing, low and certain, as it had in the glass that afternoon, catching up to a thing she'd decided a half-second before her head was sure of it. The eyes found the leg because she'd handed it to them. She kept her face exactly where it was, took the woman's hand and held it the beat someone's camera wanted, took the scroll they set in her other hand — a rolled paper tied with a ribbon, absurdly light for the weight of the walk — and turned back with her name still hanging over the chairs, and the room knew, and not a soul in it could have said so out loud.

She got back to her chair and sat and put her hand at her hip again, the slit shut, decorous, a girl with a certificate. The woman read the next name. Whatever the room had understood it folded up and put away, as rooms do, and moved on — and only she was left with it, running under the dress in the good light while her heart went like she'd run the whole way.

Cassie found her in the crush after, by the coffee no one was drinking, and took her by both shoulders and read her fast and whole. "Honor student," she said, dry, and meaning it; her eyes had gone bright at the corners as they had in the hall the day the letter came, which she'd deny to the grave.

"How did I look?" It was the only thing in her head. It had been the only thing in her head the whole walk.

"Like a movie star. Somebody had to document it." Cassie turned Vee's phone around. Vee, mid-stride, caught in the long step: the leg out of the slit to the hip, the bare shoulder, the burgundy taking the window light like it was lit from inside, her chin level and her face giving away absolutely nothing. It looked like a photograph of a woman who had every part of it handled, because she had.

Vee took the phone and knew to the heartbeat what second it was — the one where she'd decided, and her body had come up to meet the decision. None of that was in the picture. Or all of it was, and only she would ever see it there.

She looked a moment longer, and then, because he'd been sorry to miss it and this was the nearest thing to handing it to him, she sent it to Pace — the whole frame, uncropped, the leg and the shoulder and the woman who had it handled. In his Berkeley hotel between sessions, he'd open it and watch the dress do the single thing he'd built it to do and hadn't got to see. He would have no idea what else was in the frame. That part was hers.

"Come on, streetlight." Cassie steered her by the elbow toward the doors. "I'll buy you a terrible sandwich and you can be famous at me."

Vee went, the scroll light in one hand and the phone warm in the other, the high room emptying of its light behind them. The danger of it was behind her now — she could walk any way she pleased out to the car, so she let the slit swing on her bare leg with every step and didn't make herself small about it, and couldn't have told you, if you'd stopped her in the doorway and asked, whether that was pride or only the same heat still running, or whether, by now, the two came to the same thing.
