# Vee — Christmas

*Vee POV, close third. New Year's Eve, Christmas break — her childhood room, Ohio. One panel of the break triptych. Vee alone.*

---

Vee's bedroom chilled at night because her parents were careful with money and at night you burrowed under the blankets. Her sheets were pulled up to her chin to keep warm, smelling of the fabric softener her mother always used mingled with the old lady scent of the lavender plugins in every room. She kept her knees a bit bent as she had since the end of middle school when her legs had outgrown her bed.

The house had gone down early. Her mother worked the floor at the hospital and slept like the dead when she could get it; her father was up at five for the shop, holiday or not; by ten the hall was dark and the house cooling as the furnace went into savings mode, and the year had forty minutes left with each one creeping by her.

She wasn't tired enough to sleep. There was the ball drop downstairs if she wanted it — the crowds, the confetti, the countdown to somebody else's midnight — but going down to watch it alone was worse than lying up here in the cold, so she stayed, and waited for sleep to come and muffle the hour, and it didn't.

The room did not know her. It had known Vivienne — knew her still, in high resolution, and loved her. The ribbons pinned in their bunting around the corkboard, the round ones, first and second and the gold-fringed best-in-show. The synchronized-swimming trophies on the shelf, the little gold girls frozen mid-lift with their toes pointed. The clarinet in its case in the closet where it had been since senior year. The faces on the board, the girls she'd once have said were her whole life and who hadn't texted in a year. The room kept all of it dusted and had no idea who was in the bed. It was a small room. It had never once felt small — it had been the whole world — but now, on her back, the ceiling came down close over her. She'd been breathing bigger air for months, and come home to a place that no longer fit her.

The days behind her ran together beige. The tree with the same ornaments in the same places; the wrapping at the kitchen table with her mother, the tape and the scissors and the news muttering low, always something dire; the tidying that was never finished and never needed doing. And the food — the casserole and the potatoes and the good meat her mother made because it was Christmas and it was love and had never once been the thing Vee was hungry for, not then and not now; she just hadn't had the word for it then. Wheel of Fortune in the evenings, her father calling the letters, her mother getting the puzzle three squares before the board. And the question, come at from a fresh angle every day and always the same underneath — *how's your young man, when do we get to meet him, is it serious* — landing every time on a door she kept shut and smiled in front of. She had no answer that would fit in this house. She was no longer sure she had one that fit anywhere.

She reached over the side of the bed to the floor where the phone lay charging, brought it up into the dark, turned it low. Nothing from him. She hadn't thought there would be, and there wasn't. She went up through the old messages the way you press a bruise, the small warm things he'd sent, none of them saying anything, all of them him, and got as far as the empty little field and held her thumb over it. There was nothing to put there. She'd learned that already, in another bed, in another cold — there was no message you could send that came back the shape of the thing you needed. Anything she typed would be small, and be answered small and warm, and leave her exactly here. She started one anyway, *are you*—, and took it back letter by letter, and was glad, in the sick way, that he was not a man who lived on his phone, that he'd likely never see the three dots start and stop. He left you the room. He always left you the room. There was no one in it with her.

She went into the pictures instead. The two of them on his couch, her arm out with the phone, both of them caught laughing at nothing and her face open in a way it never got to be in this house. Herself in the black dress the girl down the hall had lent her, the night of the dance, before it went wrong. And the induction — the one Cassie caught, mid-stride in the long step, the leg out of the slit clear to the hip and the burgundy lit like it held its own light, and at the end of the leg the heel Randi had knelt and buckled with two fingers, four inches, a fine dark strap and not much else. All of her handed to the frame at once. She'd sent that one to Pace whole. The one downstairs on her mother's phone she'd cut off at the waist herself before she let it go — scissored the leg away, the exact thing that had made her feel like herself, because her father was going to see it and her father did not need to know what the dress Pace built for her actually did. Same picture, one house, and she was the one who had divided it. She looked at all of it a while in the dark, and put it away.

Randi's feed was another country and dressed like one. The top of the run, all that white, Randi in her goggles and a grin. The chalet by evening, its windows gold against the blue of the snow. Randi dressed for dinner somewhere with a low warm light, bare shoulders and the good earrings, the mountains gone black in the glass behind her. Vee lay under the shelf of little gold swimmers and looked at it and wanted it, wanted every part of it, and it did not matter that she knew what had bought the plane. She knew. She'd been in the glass room when the name came up and the color went straight out of Randi's face; she knew whose money it was and that it was the kind of money that put that look on a person. And she envied the mountains anyway, because from a twin bed with Wheel of Fortune still going in her ears, even a poisoned Gstaad was more life than she had.

She typed the message before she'd decided to. Not the thing — she never said the thing, hadn't in the glass room and hadn't after, had folded it away and let Randi cover it and never once let on she'd seen. Just: *thinking about you 💛 hope it's beautiful out there. i'm here if you want to talk. or not.* She read it twice for anything that gave her away, found nothing, and sent it. Put the phone face-down. Turned it back over to be sure it had gone. It had.

Nothing came back. The minutes went by and nothing came, and she set it down and picked it up and set it down, and the year wore toward its end with the field empty.

It lit the ceiling near midnight.

*you ANGEL 😇 it's honestly a fairytale out here — dad's got us in this insane chalet, mountains for days, i'm never coming home. miss your face most though 💋*

So she was fine. Better than fine — a fairy tale, a chalet, mountains for days, while Vee lay here. She read it twice and was glad and felt the twelve hours it had taken sit inside the words like the distance they were, six time zones and a mountain range, Randi asleep now in all that gold. She'd reached across it and touched the glass and it had come back warm and left her nothing to hold. Both of them dark the same night, then — the one who would not reach and the one she could not reach — both of them in the small lit rectangle in her hand, and neither of them here.

And into the space the phone left came the thing she'd held off all day. She did not know what she was to him. She did not know, lying here, whether they were still anything at all. She went back through the last night — the fight, if it was a fight, her mother's question in her own mouth by then, *what am I to you,* and his answer warm and level and wrong, and then the quiet that neither of them broke, no answer and no coming-after, and nothing since, not a word across the whole break. Maybe it was over. Maybe he thought it was and she didn't. Maybe he thought nothing had happened at all. She turned it over and it would not come out even.

And with no name to put on herself there was none to put on him. He could be anyone's, anywhere, and she had no standing to so much as ask. She knew one thing and it was the wrong thing to know. He had cooked for Sheri at Thanksgiving. *She's alone,* he'd said, and Vee had let it be true, had folded it small as pity because it was pity, in November, when she was the one he cooked for. But about Christmas he'd said nothing at all — a whole week she couldn't see into. She could see him at the stove, though. She could always see him at the stove: the care in his hands that was the same care in his hands, the plate set down before you'd said you were hungry. And now it was set down for someone, and she didn't get to know who.

So she gave him one. The face came up on its own, as the worst ones do — the blonde from the dance, the pretty one at the bar with her drink and her *all us girls love him* — and Vee built her out into everything she herself was not: blonde where Vee was red, brown and smooth where Vee was pale, long and lean and cut like a photograph where Vee was a body a dress had to be *built* for. She handed Pace the exact opposite of herself and named it the thing he would want the moment he was free of her, and she believed it, lying there, the way you believe a thing at midnight in a childhood bed.

She reached the other way to get out from under it. Reached for the best night she had, to put herself back inside it — the first one, after, wrapped in the top sheet on the stool at his counter with the wine she hadn't drunk earlier, watching him build the fra diavolo from where they'd left it, the oil, the garlic and pepper blooming, the tomatoes going in, the kitchen smelling right and her body quiet and the evening having used her well; the sheet slipping and neither of them fixing it; nothing that needed saying. She went into it to be warm.

And it turned while she was in it. The one on the stool in the falling sheet stopped being her. The hair went gold and the leg went long and brown and the blonde sat where Vee had sat in the wine light, the sheet slipping off a body that had never in its life needed building for, and Pace at the stove not turning around, the garlic and pepper blooming, the tomatoes going in, the kitchen smelling right. She could not keep her own night. She had reached for the one thing that was hers and it had taken the sheet off her and put the other one under it while she watched.

People had told her things. The bartender, the older one, closing the place down that night without looking up from the glasses — *he doesn't take advantage, I'd have heard; Pace don't bring dates; wasn't him they were jealous of, honey.* And Randi — *he doesn't sulk, he leaves you the room.* She reached for those too. But they had only ever held her up while she was the one, while she knew she was the one, and now she did not know she was anything, and they went straight through her hands. The floor was gone. There was nothing under the thought at all.

The phone said 11:52. She thought, the way you think a thing to make it a plan, that she'd done without him long before she ever had him — this room had done without him, this whole small safe life had — and she could do it again. She lay in the warm lavender dark of a room that loved a girl who no longer lived inside her, and did not believe it now any more than she had the last time she'd made herself say it. The year would come in with Randi already penciled into it — school back, the brunches back, a standing morning and a standing table — and Pace a blank, an empty week wherever she tried to set him, nothing scheduled, nothing promised, no place she could be sure of him.

Somewhere down the street a few small poppers went off, and a dog, and then the year was over. She had no one to turn to. She lay still with her feet at the footboard and let herself have the thing everyone else was having somewhere tonight — arms around her, his arms, the strong warm weight of them drawing her back into him — and it came, and it would not hold, because in the same breath she could not keep from it: whose he had them around instead. The wanting and the fear in the one image, and no way on earth to know which was the true one.

She'd have Randi back on Monday. It was the only sure thing the year had handed her across the turn — a morning, a table, a face that would light up and call her gorgeous and mean it. And Pace came with none of that: no hour she could name, no room he'd surely be in. A date to see the one and nothing at all with the other. She lay there in a smaller self she couldn't feel she had shrunk back into, gone somewhere without any sense that she had left, holding both of them and setting neither down, and thought, *and what about Pace,* and had no next thought to follow it. She was too tired for the next thought. She was still reaching when it went out from under her, down into a thin and turning sleep, while the furnace ran and the lavender came up sweet off the pillow and the room kept its watch over the girl who was gone.
