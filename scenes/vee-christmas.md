# Vee — Christmas

*Vee POV, close third. New Year's Eve, Christmas break — her childhood room, Ohio. One panel of the break triptych. Vee alone.*

---

Vee's bedroom chilled at night because her parents were careful with money and at night you burrowed under the blankets. Her sheets were pulled up to her chin to keep warm, smelling of the fabric softener her mother always used mingled with the old lady scent of the lavender plugins in every room. She kept her knees a bit bent as she had since the end of middle school when her legs had outgrown her bed.

The house had gone down early. Her mother worked the floor at the hospital and slept like the dead when she could get it; her father was up at five for the shop, holiday or not; by ten the hall was dark and the house cooling as the furnace went into savings mode, and the year had forty minutes left with each one creeping by her.

She wasn't tired enough to sleep. There was the ball drop downstairs if she wanted it — the crowds, the confetti, the countdown to somebody else's midnight — but going down to watch it alone was worse than lying up here in the cold, so she stayed, and waited for sleep to come and muffle the hour, and it didn't.

The room did not know her. It had known Vivienne — knew her still, in high resolution, and loved her. The ribbons pinned in their bunting around the corkboard, the round ones, first and second and the gold-fringed best-in-show. The synchronized-swimming trophies on the shelf, the little gold girls frozen mid-lift with their toes pointed. The clarinet in its case in the closet where it had been since senior year. The faces on the board, the girls she'd once have said were her whole life and who hadn't texted in a year. The room kept all of it dusted and had no idea who was in the bed. It was a small room. It had never once felt small — it had been the whole world — but now, on her back, the ceiling came down close over her. She'd been breathing bigger air for months, and come home to a place that no longer fit her.

The days behind her ran together beige. The tree with the same ornaments in the same places; the wrapping at the kitchen table with her mother, the tape and the scissors and the news muttering low, always something dire; the tidying that was never finished and never needed doing. And the food — the casserole and the potatoes and the good meat her mother made because it was Christmas and it was love and had never once been the thing Vee was hungry for, not then and not now; she just hadn't had the word for it then. Wheel of Fortune in the evenings, her father calling the letters, her mother getting the puzzle three squares before the board. And the question, come at from a fresh angle every day and always the same underneath — *how's your young man, when do we get to meet him, is it serious* — landing every time on a door she kept shut and smiled in front of. She had no answer that would fit in this house. She was no longer sure she had one that fit anywhere.

She reached over the side of the bed to the little nightstand where her phone lay charging, brought it up, searched the screen. Nothing from him. She hadn't thought there would be, and there wasn't. She went up through the old messages the way you worry a sore tooth — the small warm things he'd sent, checking she'd gotten back to the dorm, asking how her day had been — and got as far as the little field and held her thumb over it. There was nothing to put there. She'd learned that already, in another bed, in another cold room: there was no message she could send that came back in the shape of what she needed. Anything she typed would be small, and given how she'd left things she didn't even know if he'd answer, or want to. She started one anyway, *are you*—, and took it back letter by letter, glad he wasn't a man who lived on his phone, that he'd likely never see the three dots start and stop. He left you the room. He always left you the room — a thing she'd appreciated once, and hated now.

She went to the pictures instead. The two of them on his couch, her arm out with the phone, both of them caught laughing at nothing and her face open in a way it never got to be in this house. Herself in the keyhole shirt and boots she'd borrowed off the dorm, the night of the dance, before it went wrong. Before she made it wrong. And the induction — the one Cassie caught, mid-stride in the long step, the leg out of the slit clear to the hip and the burgundy lit like it held its own light, and at the end of the leg the heel Randi had knelt and buckled to her foot. All of her handed to the frame at once. In the picture she had every part of it handled — her body come up to meet the moment — and for once it was out where a whole room could see, which had never happened before. She'd sent that one to Pace whole, all of her. The one downstairs on her mother's phone she'd cut off at the waist herself before she let it go — scissored the leg away, the exact thing that had made her feel like herself, because her parents did not need to know what the dress Pace had built for her actually did. Same picture, one house, and she was the one who had divided it. She looked at all of it a while in the dark, and put it away.

Randi's feed was another country and dressed like another world. The top of the ski run, all that white, Randi in her goggles and a grin. The chalet by evening, its windows golden against the blue of the snow. Randi dressed for dinner somewhere fancy, bare shoulders, white gold bracelet, and the solitaire at her throat, the mountains gone black in the glass behind her. Vee lay under the shelf of the little gold swimmers and looked at it and wanted it, every part of it, and it did not matter that she knew what had bought the dream. She knew. She'd been in the glass room when the name came up and Randi blanched; she knew whose money it was. And she envied the mountains anyway, because from a twin bed with Wheel of Fortune still going in her ears, even a poisoned Gstaad was more life than she had.

She wrote to Randi, warm, and made sure it demanded nothing — an open hand offered. Not the thing — she never said the thing, hadn't in the glass room and hadn't after, had folded it away and let Randi cover it and never once let on she'd seen. Just: *thinking about you 💛 hope it's beautiful out there. i'm here if you want to talk. or not.* She read it twice for anything that gave her away, found nothing, and sent it. Put the phone face-down. Turned it back over to be sure it had gone. It had.

Nothing came back. The minutes went by and nothing came, and she set it down and picked it up and set it down, and the year wore toward its end with the field empty.

She did the math without deciding to. Six hours ahead — going on six in the morning there, and Randi would still be up; glamorous, wild Randi wouldn't go to bed on New Year's Eve, not out there. Past that Vee couldn't see, and filled it in as she always had to with Randi, out of Randi's own telling, which came at her like something off a screen: a white-tie crush where the whole room knew Randi's name, or Randi slipped off somewhere with a man out of the stories — the whole lit spectacle of a life lived out loud, in front of everyone. That was the part she envied. Not the nights; her nights she couldn't have found words for, and wouldn't have traded. But they happened behind his door, the two of them and no one to know, and the one time she'd asked to be taken out among people he'd taken her and it had gone badly. Randi lived where everyone could see. Vee kept the best of hers behind a shut door.

It lit the ceiling near midnight.

*you ANGEL 😇 it's honestly a fairytale out here — dad's got us in this insane chalet, mountains for days, i'm never coming home. miss your face most though, gorgeous 💋*

She was fine. Better than fine — off somewhere in all that snow with a man she'd just met, a devastatingly handsome billionaire, a weekend so wild Vee wouldn't recognize half of what they did, and it sounded wonderful to her, while she lay here alone with her flannel sheets and old lady air freshener. And Randi had taken the time out of whatever amazing thing she was doing to write back to Vee. Vee thought about how fun her brunches with Randi were. Randi, the only girl she could talk to about real things.

The real things they talked about were mostly Pace. She wished she could talk to Randi now. She did not know what she was to him. She did not know, lying here, whether they were still anything at all. She went back through the last night — the fight, if it was a fight, her mother's question in her own mouth by then, *what am I to you,* and his answer warm and level and wrong, and then the quiet that neither of them broke, no answer and no coming-after, and nothing since, not a word across the whole break. Maybe it was over. Maybe he thought it was and she didn't. Maybe he thought nothing had happened at all. She turned it over and it would not come out even.

And with no name to put on herself there was none to put on him. He could be anyone's, anywhere, and she had no standing to so much as ask. She knew one thing and it was the wrong thing to know. He had cooked for Sheri at Thanksgiving. *She's alone,* he'd said, and Vee had accepted it then, had folded it away as pity, in November, when she was the one he cooked for. But about Christmas he'd said nothing at all — a whole week she couldn't see into. She could see him at the stove, though. She could always see him at the stove: the garlic going into the oil, the smell of it coming up and drawing the hunger out of her before she'd have called it hunger, the plate set down only once she was aching for it — the same building, then giving, that his hands worked on her in the dark. And now he was doing it for someone, and she didn't get to know who.

So she found a who. The face came up on its own — some pretty girl from the dance hall — and Vee built her from spite: blonde, tan, a fashion model's body where Vee's was a stripper's. She handed Pace the exact opposite of herself and named it the thing he would want the moment he was free of her, and she believed it, lying there, the way you believe a thing at midnight, cold and alone on the one night you're not supposed to be.

She reached the other way to get out from under it. Reached for the best night she had, to put herself back inside it — the first one, after, wrapped in the top sheet on the stool at his counter with the wine she hadn't drunk earlier, watching him build the fra diavolo from where they'd left it, the oil, the garlic and pepper blooming, the tomatoes going in, the kitchen smelling right and her body quiet and the evening having used her well; the sheet slipping and him seeing and her not fixing it; nothing that needed saying. She went into it to be warm.

And it turned while she was in it. The one on the stool in the falling sheet stopped being her. The hair went gold and the leg went tan and the blonde sat where Vee had sat in the wine light, the sheet slipping off a body that had never in its life needed building for, and Pace at the stove looking and smiling, the garlic and pepper blooming, the tomatoes going in, the kitchen smelling right. She could not keep her own night. She had reached for the one thing that was hers and it had taken the sheet off her and put the other one under it while she watched.

People had told her things. The bartender, the older lady, closing the place down that night without looking up from the glasses — *he doesn't take advantage, I'd have heard; Pace don't bring dates; wasn't him they were jealous of, honey.* And Randi — *he doesn't sulk, he leaves you the room.* She reached for those too. But they had only ever held her up while she was the one, while she knew she was the one, and now she did not know she was anything, and they went straight through her hands. The floor was gone. There was nothing under the thought at all.

The phone said 11:52. She thought, the way you think a thing to make it a plan, that she'd done without him long before she ever had him — this room had done without him, this whole small safe life had — and she could do it again. She lay in the warm lavender dark of a room that loved a girl who no longer lived inside her, and did not believe it now any more than she had the last time she'd made herself say it. The year would come in with Randi already penciled into it — school back, the brunches back, a standing morning and a standing table — and Pace a blank, an empty week wherever she tried to set him, nothing scheduled, nothing promised, no place she could be sure of him.

Somewhere down the street a few small poppers went off, and a dog, and then the year was over. She had no one to turn to. She lay still with her feet at the footboard and let herself have the thing everyone else was having somewhere tonight — arms around her, his arms, the strong warm weight of them drawing her back into him — and it came, and it would not hold, because in the same breath she could not keep from it: whose he had them around instead. The wanting and the fear in the one image, and no way on earth to know which was the true one.

She'd have Randi back on Monday. It was the only sure thing the year had handed her across the turn — a morning, a table, a face that would light up and call her gorgeous and mean it. And Pace came with none of that: no hour she could name, no room he'd surely be in. A date to see the one and nothing at all with the other. She lay there in a smaller self she couldn't feel she had shrunk back into, gone somewhere without any sense that she had left, holding both of them and setting neither down, and thought, *and what about Pace,* and had no next thought to follow it. She was too tired for the next thought. She was still reaching when it went out from under her, down into a thin and turning sleep, while the furnace ran and the lavender came up sweet off the pillow and the room kept its watch over the girl who was gone.
