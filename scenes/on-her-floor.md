# On Her Floor

After a few sets they came off the floor flushed and laughing, holding each other up by the forearms, and went to the bar for something cold. Randi ordered a margarita, and Vee — watching the salt, the pale cloudy green of it, the sweet-and-sour that would carry the liquor down so gently you barely met it — said she'd have the same. She had meant to drink champagne tonight; she'd decided on it days ago, the way you decide what kind of girl you'll be at a party you're nervous about. She was glad already she'd followed Randi instead.

The music made talking a contact sport. You got a sentence in the gap between songs, and after that there was only the cupped hand, the mouth brought to the ear, the half-shouted nothing you laughed at because the laughing was the point. Randi said things into Vee's ear and Vee felt the words more than heard them, the warm shape of the breath, and said things back, and they sat close the way the room made everyone sit close. At some point a second margarita was in front of her; she hadn't seen Randi order it. She had never been so easy in her own skin in a room full of strangers.

They'd kept to the edge of the floor — the middle was a crush, but the edge gave you room to move, and it looked out over the long tables and the clots of people who'd come to watch more than dance. Which was the trouble with the skirt. It climbed. On the floor it climbed by the song, and every inch it took was an inch handed to all those faces past the edge, the ones not dancing, the ones with nothing to do but look. Vee kept a hand low on her thigh to hold the hem down and brought her arms up only in quick bursts before snatching them back. She'd put the green thong on that night, after her shower, getting ready for these exact people, when it had been nobody's business but hers. By the hour, on this floor, it was coming nearer to being everybody's. She got good, over the night, at the arithmetic of it — how high was still a joke, how high stopped being one — and she kept half an eye down and a hand always ready.

Then the song came on that the whole room had been waiting for. A shriek went up; the floor pulled bodies out of every corner; and Randi was on her feet with both of Vee's hands in hers. "Oh — no, you can't miss this," she said, walking backward, pulling, and Vee went, because the alternative was not going, and there was no version of the night in which she chose that.

She'd been dancing small for an hour, elbows in, the radius of herself kept modest, and Randi wouldn't have it. "No. Like *this*." She spun in behind her and put both hands warm on the curve of Vee's hips, the roundness of them, and moved them — wider, looser, an arc Vee would never have given herself — so the dancing got bigger and the skirt got braver in exact measure. And Randi's hands stayed where they were. There was nowhere left for Vee's own hand to go that wouldn't have looked like just what it was.

The song climbed into the part where everyone's arms go up, the whole floor a field of them, and Randi's hands left her hips and went up the sides of her — slow, over the thin fabric, past the soft give beneath her arms, along the undersides to her wrists — and lifted them, and held them in the air. Her thighs were open to the room now, the iridescent skirt giving them almost nothing, and there was nothing Vee could do about it, because Randi had her hands. She felt Randi fit against her back, the damp silk of her, the heat through it, and Randi did not so much keep the beat as work it into her — a small restless wriggle of hips that traveled up through Vee whether she wanted it or not — and somewhere under the noise and the heat and the two margaritas, her body was working through something her mind had decided not to check.

Then Randi turned her — a flourish, hand finding hand, her fingers going through Vee's and closing, slick palm to slick palm — and danced her out to arm's length and pumped their joined hands back and forth, big and ridiculous and joyful, and ran Vee's whole body on the end of that grip, and Vee let herself be run, laughing, helpless with it. The hem was high. She could feel how high by the cool that reached the very top of her thigh, the give of air where no air should have been, and she knew that she had crossed some line a few inches back and was still going, because the only way to stop was to let go of Randi's hands and walk out of the bright middle of it and be, again, a careful person at the edge of a party. She did not let go.

The song broke. The floor cheered itself hoarse. Randi reeled her in by the hands she was still holding — close, closer than even the noise required — and put her mouth to Vee's ear, so that Vee felt the lips, the actual lips, brush the rim of it, and a shiver went down the side of her neck and into her shoulder before she could rule on whether she'd allowed it.

"Your panties," Randi said, warm, amused, only for her, "are showing."

Vee's hands came down and dragged the hem to where it should have been, too late, far too late. How long had it been up — a whole song? Longer? The edge of the floor was all faces, and she'd been turned to hand every one of them the back of her, the green, the almost-nothing. What had they seen? How much had she shown? The thong didn't even try to hide the roundness of her bottom. How much of her center had they seen? Some girl dancing up past the edge of her own skirt, flushed, letting another girl run her around by the wrists — what kind of floozie did that, in front of a room, and didn't even feel it going? She felt it now. It climbed her face, hot, and she waited for it to be only awful. It was not only awful. Underneath the shame, low, where she didn't look, something turned the heat over into the other thing, the thing she had no business feeling about a roomful of strangers and the back of a borrowed skirt — and she stood there in the broken-off music with her hem in both fists, found out twice over: once to the room, and once, worse, to herself.
