# The Top Drawer

*Vee POV. Early November, the weekend after We Find Out, into the next. Three movements.*

---

**i.**

The sheet had gone cool everywhere her skin wasn't touching his, and she lay in that cooling with the whole day still loud in her body and let it come back to her in the order it had happened.

He had the door open before she reached it. A week since the last time, the longest they'd gone: he'd been away presenting at a conference, several days of it, and she'd had a brutal week on campus on top, so the midweek night they'd have stolen never happened and the whole seven days stood empty between them. He must have heard the car, because the door swung wide and then she was off the ground. He had her up under the arms and around, her feet swinging out, the porch light wheeling once behind his head, and she heard the sound that came out of her, a bright stupid sound she'd have died of anywhere else. He didn't set her down so much as let her slide the front of him until her toes found the boards, and by then her hands were already at his shirt.

They hadn't made the kitchen. They hadn't made the hall. Their coats came off them both inside the door and the rest followed, dropped where they stood, a week of wanting coming out of them at once, and even then, even with her clawing at him, he'd been slow. That was the thing she still couldn't get used to. He never went straight at his own finish. Some nights he started with his mouth, some with his hands, some he just put her where he wanted her and used the rest of himself, and she never knew which it would be, only that it always came around to her first, brought all the way over and usually more than once before he took a single thing for himself. She hadn't known a man could do that. She hadn't known a man would want to.

He'd cooked after, the way he near always did. Some nights it was a production, four pans going and a smell building she had no names for, and tonight it had been plain: salmon laid in a hot pan and turned once, a sauce gone pale green with dill, a salad with the tomatoes still warm off the sill and a mustard thing he'd whisked up in a glass that she'd wanted to drink straight. A cold white wine he said came from Greece, a little stony. She'd eaten all of it. She ate like a field hand at his table now, she who'd spent years pushing a plate away half done, and the strange part was that her clothes had gone *looser* for it. Not all of them. Her bras, the band riding higher than it used to sit. The few good pairs of underwear cut to fit her and not to a guess. Everything else she owned had been bought a size up and would keep on telling the same lie it always told, because the women who made clothes had decided a chest like hers and a backside like hers came on a heavy girl, and she was not a heavy girl, she was tall and built on a big warm scale and trained under all of it, and the racks had never once believed her and her budget had never once stretched to the tailor who would. So she swam in most of what she owned and let the bras keep the secret, that she'd been losing weight since she met him, her body quietly burning down whole weekends of him, and she'd never asked it to and didn't mind the bonus.

After they ate he'd taken her again, on the couch, unhurried, the wine still in both of them. That was another thing she was new to, that there could be an *after*, that a man might come back to her an hour later like she was something he'd been turning over the whole time the salmon cooked.

Now the house ticked and settled and he slept, flat on his back, gone the moment it was over, one arm flung up above his head. She turned on her side to watch him in the dark. The broad chest he kept hidden all day under shirts a size too big rose and fell, slow, and the old scar high on the inside of his thigh was a paler line in the gloom that she'd still never asked about and still meant to keep. She was happy. She set the word down plainly, lying there, happier than she'd known a person was let to be, and underneath the happiness, holding it up, was the other word she'd been carrying around all week.

She loved him. There. She'd said it, or not said it, but let it be true in her own chest where she couldn't take it back. She loved him, and he had never once said it to her, and she lay there and counted all the ways he'd said it without saying it: the door, the spin, the slowness, the salmon, the warm tomatoes, the coming back to her. A man didn't do all of that for a girl he was only passing a season with. He had to feel it. He felt it in everything he did and said it in nothing, and she found, tonight, that she could live clear inside the everything-he-did and not go hungry for the saying.

She moved closer and tucked herself along his side and put her mouth up near his ear.

"Peter," she said. Barely the breath of it, to the sleeping shape of him.

He didn't stir. She hadn't meant him to. She shut her eyes against the warm of him and let the day finish letting go of her, and was under before she could think one more thing.

**ii.**

In the morning her hair had kept the whole night in it.

She stood at the sink in his robe with the belt doubled twice around her and still trailing, and worked at the wreck of it the only way it could be done, one piece at a time. Brush first, down through the worst of the knots until it ran clean. Then the pins, held in her teeth and set one at a time, lifting and turning the dark red of it up off her neck. It looked, in the glass, like something a surgeon might step back from and judge would live. Then the scrunchy, deep green with the small white dots, one of the dozens he'd bought her so she'd never be without, off the form by the tap and twisted twice around the knot.

The counter in front of her was a disaster. It was the only bathroom he had, and her whole kit had colonized it, the brush and the loose pins scattered, a fallen scrunchy, her face cream open, two bottles she'd stood up and one she hadn't, all of it flung across a stretch of counter that every other hour of the week stood bare and wiped and held nothing but his razor squared to the edge. She'd started bringing the bag, finally, gotten over herself and brought it, and the bag had done this, had thrown her whole disordered self across the one clean surface in a clean man's house.

He came in behind her. She felt the warmth of him before the door, and then his arms came in under hers and crossed at her front and drew her back against his chest, and his mouth found the side of her neck, slow, no part of it asking for a thing. She let her weight go back into him and watched the two of them in the glass, her with her arms still up in her hair, him folded around her with his eyes half shut, and she was so full of the night and the morning at once that her throat pulled tight.

She watched his eyes open. She watched them move, in the mirror, off her face and down to the counter, to the brush and the pins and the bottles and the spill of her, and hold there a second, taking it in.

Something small went through her. Not the deep thing. Only the surface worry of a guest who's left a ring on good wood, the wish not to be a trouble to him, this contained and careful man in his own house, his razor squared to its edge, and here was all of her flung across his order like she owned the place. She held still and waited to see what he'd do.

He did nothing. His eyes came back up and found hers in the glass, and he smiled, the small private one, and put his mouth back to her shoulder where it had been, and that was all of it. Not one word. She let her breath out and leaned into him and let the worry go, mostly, and carried only the smallest tail of it out of the room with her. *Had he minded.* She couldn't tell. He hadn't said. He never said.

**iii.**

The next Friday the scrunchies weren't on the counter.

She had her hands up in her hair before it registered, reaching for the green by the tap out of pure habit, and her fingers closed on nothing. The form was gone. The counter was bare, wiped, his razor at its edge, and for a second the bottom went out of her morning. He'd cleared it. He'd minded after all, and quietly, the way he did everything, he'd gathered up her sprawl and put it somewhere out of his sight, and now she was going to have to learn where a guest's things went once the guest had been too much.

She opened the top drawer to look.

It had been his. She knew it had, because she'd seen into it once, the spare blades and the comb and the small ordered ranks of a careful man. None of that was in it now. It held her form of green scrunchies, and her loose pins gathered into a little dish, and her face cream stood upright, and a clear run of liner with room to spare, room for more of her, laid out like something prepared and waiting.

She pulled the drawer below it. There was everything of his, the blades and the comb and the rest, pressed in and fitted too tight together, made to share the one drawer that had plainly been built to hold half of what was crammed in it now. He'd folded his whole ordered self down into a single drawer. He'd given her the top one. The easy one. The one your hand goes to first.

There was no note. There was never a note.

She stood with a hand on each open drawer and understood that she'd asked him a question last weekend without meaning to, standing right here with her hair full of pins, and that he hadn't answered it the way she'd waited all week to be answered. He hadn't told her it didn't matter. He'd gone and made her a place instead, squared his own life down small to clear it, and left it for her to find with nothing said over it at all.

She took a green scrunchy out of her drawer. Hers. Put her hair up, and went to find him.
