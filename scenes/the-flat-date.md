# The Flat Date

*Randi's POV. Christmas break. The conventionally perfect man and the nothing he produces — the mirror aperture that confirms the configuration has no substitute.*

---

Afterward she stayed against him. He was warm and heavy and already going under, and she pressed the length of herself to the length of him and went still, as you go still for a photograph, waiting to see what the holding would develop into. She waited a while. His breathing lengthened and slowed and became sleep. Nothing developed.

It had been good. She could not have said otherwise; her body was still loose with how good, still humming in the ordinary places. And it had meant no more than a good sandwich eaten fast on a busy afternoon: the pleasure real, complete, and gone before the plate was cleared, nothing left behind it to keep. She lay there and tried to make it be more than that, and it would not be more than that.

She was warm only where she touched him. Along that one seam, her chest to his side, her thigh over his, she had his heat. Everywhere else she was cold, the ordinary cold of a room at night, except the cold did not belong to the room. It was hers. She had brought it in with her and she would carry it back out.

When she was sure he was under she got up without waking him. He lay naked on the tangled sheets, and she could see where she had left herself on him: the print of her mouth on his chest, and lower, a ring of her lipstick, faint already, where she had taken him in her mouth and felt her own mouth do it from a long way off. Her body had spent itself on him, and she had felt none of it leave.

At the mirror her mouth was bare, the color of it left on him; the rest of the face had thinned and smeared. The eyeliner had held. Of course it had. Nothing had happened to her eyes.

She began to put the face back on, and it came back as it always did, even, exact, the color good, and there was no one behind it, and she studied that with the flat interest of a woman reading a result she already knew. This was the best the acceptable world could do for her. Not a poor version of it. The best: a faultless man asleep in a faultless room, everything done to her and done well, and it had touched the surface of her and gone no deeper.

Pace on his worst night reached further than this man had managed at his best. She did not have to want that to be true for it to be true, and she did not look at what he'd had to see in her to reach so far.

And Vee. Something came off Vee across a table on a nothing Tuesday that this room could not counterfeit one thread of, and her whole body knew it, and she knew exactly what it was.

She could tell her. The thought sat where it always sat, and she took it out and turned it over. She could say it plainly, over eggs, the way Vee said every plain true thing. What she could not do was picture Vee's face while she said it. That was the one scenario she could not run to the end, and she had always been able to run a scenario to the end. The rest she could run. Vee would not cut her loose; Vee was too kind for that, would be gentle, would try, would find something soft to say. But they would not be this again. The talk that ran hot and easy between them would learn to watch itself. She would be trading the most important hour of her week for the terrified hope that Vee might feel it too.

So she would not tell her. There was the other way, the one that never asked Randi to say the word and never made Vee decide what to do with it. Let the thing keep the shape it was already taking. Let Vee arrive at it on her own. Keep the brunches exactly as they were. She could have the aliveness and keep the friendship both, as long as she never once said it out loud.

The nothing asleep behind her asked for none of that and gave none of it back. She fixed the last of her hair, turned off the light, and went back to the bed and lay down against him again, in the warmth she could borrow and could not make, and stayed.
