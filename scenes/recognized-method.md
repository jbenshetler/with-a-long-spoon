"Everything was fine." There was no edge in it. He wasn't cornering her; he genuinely wanted to know, she could see that he genuinely wanted to know, which was worse. "We were dancing. The clean machine had no part for this. She stopped. She couldn't say the like-what. It was too ugly to set down in his front hall, the thing the blonde had built out of his kindness, the idea that getting a drunk girl safely home was a thing he did to them and not for them.

"And it got to me anyway," she said. She handed me a lie and I picked it up because some part of me was just standing there waiting for one."

Her eyes had gone. He had been quiet the whole way through, and now he crossed the small distance she'd been keeping, and he didn't say it's fine, didn't reach for any of the things that would have closed it over too easily. He took her face in both hands, warm, and tipped it down a little, because he had to tip it down now, and looked at her.

"Thank you for telling me what she said," he said.

Which was such a strange thing to be thanked for that she laughed, wetly, once, and that was the end of the apology, though the evening went on a long time after it. What the contrition turned into somewhere along the way, first lighter and then not light at all, the way he took her to bed eventually, none of that belonged to the apology anymore. She stayed. She had not packed a bag; she had not let herself think past the front step, because to pack would have been to admit she'd come hoping for this, and she had only had him the once, and the hope was too big and too new to say even to herself. And still she stayed. The last clear thing she thought before the night took her under was that she'd carried the whole weight of it up the hill to his door for two days and he had simply reached over and lifted it out of her hands, like the pie.
